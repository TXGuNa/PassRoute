Question 1

Driving at dusk, you approach an intersection where the sign facing you is badly faded and you can no longer read any of its lettering. You can still clearly see that the sign has eight sides. What must you do at this intersection?

  1. Come to a complete stop, because an eight-sided (octagonal) sign always means STOP. ✓
  2. Slow down and yield the right of way, stopping only if other traffic is present.
  3. Slow down and proceed with caution, since the shape only warns of a hazard ahead.
  4. Treat it as a school crossing and be ready for children entering the roadway.
Correct answer: A. Come to a complete stop, because an eight-sided (octagonal) sign always means STOP.
Why: The octagon is the only eight-sided sign and always means STOP, so the shape alone tells you to come to a full stop even when the lettering is unreadable. Yielding is signaled by an inverted triangle, not an octagon, which makes that option the trap.
Question 2

You are merging onto a highway from an on-ramp and see a red-and-white sign shaped like an upside-down (inverted) triangle facing you. What does this sign require you to do?

  1. Come to a complete stop and remain stopped until the highway is empty in both directions
  2. Give the right of way to traffic on the highway, slowing or stopping only if necessary to do so safely ✓
  3. Proceed at the posted maximum speed because you have the right of way when merging
  4. Treat it as a warning of a hazard ahead and continue with caution without yielding
Correct answer: B. Give the right of way to traffic on the highway, slowing or stopping only if necessary to do so safely
Why: An inverted triangle is a YIELD sign: you must give the right of way to traffic already on the highway and slow or stop only if needed to merge safely. A full stop every time (A) describes an octagonal STOP sign, and a diamond-shaped warning sign (D) does not require yielding the right of way.
Question 3

You're driving on a two-lane rural highway and want to pass a slow tractor ahead. Just before you pull out, you notice a sign shaped like a triangle lying on its side (a horizontal point) posted on the LEFT shoulder of the road. What is this sign telling you?

  1. Yield to oncoming traffic before merging back into your lane
  2. A school zone or school crossing is ahead, so reduce speed
  3. You are entering a No Passing Zone and must not pass the tractor here ✓
  4. A hazard or condition is ahead, so slow down and proceed with caution
Correct answer: C. You are entering a No Passing Zone and must not pass the tractor here
Why: A pennant (a horizontal triangle) posted on the LEFT side of the road marks a No Passing Zone, so you must not pass the tractor. A yield is an inverted (upside-down) triangle, and a diamond is the warning shape, so those options confuse other sign shapes with the pennant.
Question 4

You are driving on a rural Texas highway posted at 70 mph. A sudden, heavy fog rolls in and you can barely see two car lengths ahead. Under Texas law, what speed are you permitted to drive?

  1. Slower than 70 mph — a speed that is reasonable and safe for the fog, because the posted limit is the maximum for ideal conditions only ✓
  2. Up to 70 mph, because that is the legally posted limit and you may always drive the posted speed
  3. Exactly 50 mph, because Texas law requires reducing speed by 20 mph whenever visibility is poor
  4. Up to 75 mph, since rural Texas interstates allow 75 mph regardless of conditions
Correct answer: A. Slower than 70 mph — a speed that is reasonable and safe for the fog, because the posted limit is the maximum for ideal conditions only
Why: Under Texas's prima facie (basic speed law), you must never drive faster than is reasonable and safe for existing conditions, so in heavy fog you must slow well below the posted 70 mph. Driving the full posted limit (B) is the trap: the posted number is a maximum for good conditions, not a guarantee, and the 20-mph and 75-mph figures (C, D) are real rules pulled into the wrong context.
Question 5

You turn off a posted highway onto a residential street inside a city's urban district. You see no speed limit sign anywhere along the block. What speed should you assume is the legal maximum here?

  1. 20 mph, because that is the default in any area where homes are present
  2. 30 mph, the common default limit for an urban district when no sign is posted ✓
  3. 70 mph, since that is the default for highways and no other sign overrides it
  4. There is no limit as long as you drive safely, because the sign is missing
Correct answer: B. 30 mph, the common default limit for an urban district when no sign is posted
Why: Where no sign is posted, the common default speed limit in an urban district is 30 mph, so you should treat 30 mph as the legal maximum. The 20 mph figure is the typical reduced limit for active school zones, not residential streets generally, and 70 mph is the daytime default for numbered highways, not a city street.
Question 6

You are driving on a numbered Texas highway in the middle of the afternoon. Conditions are clear and dry, but you have not seen a single posted speed limit sign for several miles. What is the maximum speed you may legally drive under the basic speed law?

  1. 75 mph, because that is the limit on all rural Texas highways
  2. 30 mph, because that is the default limit where signs are absent
  3. 70 mph, the default daytime limit on numbered highways ✓
  4. 85 mph, the highest speed allowed anywhere in Texas
Correct answer: C. 70 mph, the default daytime limit on numbered highways
Why: Where no sign is posted, the default daytime limit on numbered highways in Texas is 70 mph. The 30 mph trap is the default for urban districts, not numbered highways, and 75 and 85 mph apply only to specific posted Interstates and tollways, not as a general default.
Question 7

You arrive at an intersection and the traffic signal facing you is a steady green light. You want to turn left across oncoming traffic, but several cars are coming toward you through the intersection. What are you legally allowed to do?

  1. Proceed straight through or make your turn, but first yield to the oncoming cars and any traffic already in the intersection. ✓
  2. Turn left immediately, because a steady green light gives you a protected turn and oncoming traffic must stop for you.
  3. Stop completely behind the line and wait, treating the green like a flashing red until the oncoming cars clear.
  4. Slow down and proceed with caution without yielding, since a steady green works the same as a flashing yellow.
Correct answer: A. Proceed straight through or make your turn, but first yield to the oncoming cars and any traffic already in the intersection.
Why: A steady green means go only when the intersection is clear; you may proceed straight or turn, but you must yield to oncoming traffic and vehicles already in the intersection. Option B is the trap: only a green arrow gives a protected turn where oncoming traffic is stopped, not a steady green light.
Question 8

You are driving toward an intersection at the speed limit when the green light turns to a steady yellow. You are still about half a block away and the road behind you is clear. What should you do?

  1. Speed up to get through the intersection before the light turns red
  2. Stop before the intersection if you can do so safely, because the light is about to turn red ✓
  3. Treat it like a flashing yellow and continue through carefully without stopping
  4. Come to a full stop and then proceed when the way is clear, as you would for a flashing red
Correct answer: B. Stop before the intersection if you can do so safely, because the light is about to turn red
Why: A steady yellow light warns that the signal is about to turn red, so you should stop if you can do so safely and never speed up to beat the light. Option C describes a flashing yellow (caution) and Option D describes a flashing red (stop sign), which are different signals.
Question 9

You are approaching an intersection and the traffic light facing you turns to a steady red while you are still a short distance from the marked stop line. There is no sign prohibiting a turn, and you intend to go straight ahead. What must you do?

  1. Slow down and proceed carefully through the intersection without stopping, since the light may change soon
  2. Stop only if cross traffic is present, then continue straight through the red light
  3. Come to a complete stop behind the stop line and remain stopped until the light turns green ✓
  4. Treat the signal as a stop sign, stop fully, then proceed straight when the way is clear
Correct answer: C. Come to a complete stop behind the stop line and remain stopped until the light turns green
Why: A steady red light requires you to stop completely behind the stop line and remain stopped until the light turns green. Option D describes a flashing red light (treated like a stop sign), not a steady red, so proceeding straight on a steady red is wrong.
Question 10

You're following a sedan on a dry highway. As its rear bumper passes a roadside sign, you start counting; you reach the same sign just under 2 seconds later. What does this tell you, and what should you do?

  1. You are following too closely; ease off the gas to open the gap to at least 3 seconds. ✓
  2. Your distance is safe because 2 seconds is the recommended minimum on dry roads.
  3. Your distance is fine; the 3-second rule only applies in rain, fog, or ice.
  4. You should brake hard immediately to drop back, since you are within the truck's No-Zone.
Correct answer: A. You are following too closely; ease off the gas to open the gap to at least 3 seconds.
Why: The three-second rule says that if you reach the fixed point in less than 3 seconds after the car ahead, you are too close, so easing off the gas to restore a 3-second gap is correct. Two seconds is not the safe minimum, the 3-second rule applies in all conditions (you add more seconds for bad weather), and braking hard or worrying about a truck's No-Zone does not fit this car-following scenario.
Question 11

You normally leave a 3-second gap behind the car ahead. Tonight it starts raining hard on the freeway and a thick fog rolls in. What should you do about your following distance?

  1. Keep the same 3-second gap, since the three-second rule already covers all driving conditions
  2. Increase the gap to more than 3 seconds, because rain and fog reduce traction and visibility ✓
  3. Reduce the gap so you can keep the car ahead's taillights in clear view through the fog
  4. Switch on your high beams and hold a 2-second gap to react faster to its brake lights
Correct answer: B. Increase the gap to more than 3 seconds, because rain and fog reduce traction and visibility
Why: The three-second rule is a minimum for good conditions; in rain, fog, ice, or at high speed you must add more seconds because traction and visibility are reduced. Shortening the gap or relying on high beams (which reflect back off fog) only increases your danger.
Question 12

You are driving on a Texas highway when a pickup truck moves in close behind you and stays just a few feet off your bumper, clearly tailgating. What is the safest way to handle the space around your vehicle?

  1. Tap your brakes a few times to warn the tailgater to back off
  2. Speed up so the tailgater falls farther behind you
  3. Increase the following distance between you and the vehicle ahead so you have more room to brake gradually ✓
  4. Stay exactly three seconds behind the car ahead, since that rule already covers every situation
Correct answer: C. Increase the following distance between you and the vehicle ahead so you have more room to brake gradually
Why: When you are being tailgated, you should increase your following distance to the vehicle ahead so you can slow down or stop gradually instead of braking hard, giving the tailgater more time to react. Three seconds is only a minimum baseline and should be increased behind motorcycles, large trucks, and when tailgated, so option D is wrong, and brake-tapping or speeding up only raises the risk of a crash.
