Customs Broker License Exam: The Complete Study Guide

How the open-book Customs Broker exam works and how to prepare — classification, valuation, entry, broker compliance, and an open-book strategy that saves time.

▶ Start the free Customs Broker practice test

The U.S. Customs Broker License Examination (CBLE) is the gateway credential for anyone who wants to clear imported merchandise through U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) on behalf of importers. It has a reputation as one of the hardest professional exams in trade: most candidates fail on any given sitting. The reason is not that the questions are abstract, but that the exam is open-book and the real test is whether you can find the correct answer, fast, inside thousands of pages of dense regulation and tariff text. This guide covers what the license lets you do, how the exam is structured, the reference materials you may use, the major subject areas, and a realistic plan for passing and getting licensed.

What a licensed customs broker is and does

A customs broker is an individual, partnership, association, or corporation licensed and regulated by CBP to assist importers and exporters in meeting federal import and export requirements. Brokers are the only non-attorney private parties authorized to transact customs business on behalf of others for compensation. "Customs business" is a defined term that includes activities such as:

  • Preparing and filing entries and entry summaries (the documents declaring imported goods and the duties owed).
  • Classifying merchandise under the Harmonized Tariff Schedule and determining its dutiable value.
  • Calculating and paying duties, taxes, and fees, and filing protests, drawback claims, and other documents.
  • Advising clients on admissibility, marking, partner-government-agency requirements, and trade programs.

Passing the exam earns you an individual broker license. Most working brokers operate within a licensed broker organization (a corporation or partnership holding its own license, with a licensed individual exercising responsible supervision and control). The license carries real responsibility: brokers owe a duty of reasonable care to the government and can be penalized or have their license suspended or revoked.

Exam logistics: frequency, format, and scoring

The CBLE is administered twice a year, on the fourth Wednesday of April and the fourth Wednesday of October. Each sitting consists of 80 multiple-choice questions, each with five answer choices (A through E). You must answer at least 75 percent correctly to pass (60 of 80) within 4.5 hours — roughly three and a third minutes per question, which sounds generous until you realize a single classification question can require flipping through several chapters of the tariff and pages of rules.

The exam is now computer-based and delivered through Pearson VUE, at a testing center or, when offered, via remote proctoring. CBP charges a fee of $390. You register through CBP's online systems during a window before each exam date; for an October sitting, registration typically opens in mid-August and closes in mid-September. Always confirm the current dates and fee on CBP's website, because they can change between cycles.

Because the exam is computer-based, you can flag questions and return to them, skipping the brutal classification items on the first pass. After each exam, CBP publishes the questions and an official answer key, with a formal appeals process for defective questions. Reviewing past exams and their keys is one of the most valuable study activities available, and it is free.

The reference materials: the real skill is navigation

The CBLE is open-book, but "open-book" is misleading if you picture an easy exam. You are given access to the official references, the core of which are:

  • The Harmonized Tariff Schedule of the United States (HTSUS) — the classification and duty-rate schedule, including the General Notes and General Rules of Interpretation.
  • Title 19 of the Code of Federal Regulations (19 CFR), the customs regulations covering entry, valuation, drawback, broker conduct, and much more.
  • Supplementary materials CBP designates per exam, historically including the Right to Make Entry directive and ACE Entry Summary instructions.

The exam does not let you bring commercial study guides, handwritten notes, or other annotated materials beyond what CBP specifies. Because the references run to thousands of pages, the candidates who pass spent weeks tabbing their own copies and building a mental index of where things live — turning straight to 19 CFR Part 174 for a protest deadline without hunting. Speed of retrieval, not memorization, is the discriminating skill.

Major subject areas to master

CBP draws questions from across the regulations, but the weight clusters in a handful of areas every cycle.

Entry and entry summary procedures

This is the bread and butter of customs work and a large share of the exam. Understand who has the right to make entry, the difference between the entry (release) and the entry summary (the declaration of value and duty), the types of entry (consumption, warehouse, temporary importation under bond, transportation and exportation), and the required documents. Know the timelines cold: when the entry summary is due, when liquidation occurs, and the consequences of late filing. Many questions just test whether you can locate a deadline.

Tariff classification and the General Rules of Interpretation (GRIs)

Classification questions are the hardest and most time-consuming, and reward methodical practice. Every classification is governed by the six General Rules of Interpretation (GRIs), in order:

  • GRI 1 — classify by the terms of the headings and any relative section or chapter notes; this resolves most goods.
  • GRI 2 — incomplete or unfinished articles having the essential character of the finished good, and mixtures or combinations.
  • GRI 3 — for goods classifiable under two or more headings: (a) most specific description wins; (b) else by essential character; (c) else the heading occurring last in numerical order.
  • GRI 4 — goods most akin to the closest matching article.
  • GRI 5 — cases, containers, and packing materials.
  • GRI 6 — the same principles apply at the subheading level.

Always read the Section and Chapter Notes, because they frequently exclude goods from a heading you would otherwise pick. Practice working real merchandise descriptions from start to finish, confirming your code from the notes rather than guessing from the article description.

Customs valuation

Duty is assessed on value, so valuation is heavily tested. The primary method is transaction value: the price actually paid or payable for merchandise sold for exportation to the United States, plus additions such as packing, selling commissions, assists, royalties or license fees, and resale proceeds accruing to the seller. Know what is added, what is excluded (for example, U.S. duties and international freight when separately identified), and the fallback hierarchy when transaction value cannot apply: identical or similar merchandise, deductive value, then computed value. Related-party transactions trigger added scrutiny.

Duties, fees, drawback, and entry economics

Beyond the duty rate, expect questions on the merchandise processing fee, harbor maintenance fee, and how duties are calculated and deposited. Drawback — the refund of up to 99 percent of certain duties, taxes, and fees on exported or destroyed goods — has several types (unused merchandise, manufacturing, rejected merchandise) and strict timelines. Also study liquidation, reliquidation, and the protest process for disputing CBP decisions.

Broker compliance under 19 CFR Part 111

Part 111 governs the brokers themselves, and CBP reliably tests it. Topics include license eligibility, the duty of "responsible supervision and control," recordkeeping, the triennial status report, employee reporting, who may transact customs business, and the grounds and procedures for penalties, suspension, and revocation. Treat this part as personally relevant, not as trivia.

Trade programs, FTAs, marking, and ADD/CVD

Rounding out the exam are country-of-origin marking rules (when articles must be marked, methods, and exceptions), free trade agreements and preference programs (rules of origin, certification, and claiming preferential rates), and antidumping and countervailing duties (ADD/CVD) — extra duties on unfairly priced or subsidized imports, with their own cash-deposit rules. Bonds, partner-government-agency requirements, and intellectual-property enforcement also appear.

The modernized 19 CFR Part 111

CBP modernized the broker regulations through final rules effective in December 2022, and the exam now reflects that framework. Two changes matter most. First, CBP eliminated district permits and moved all brokers to a single national permit, so a broker can conduct customs business nationwide under one permit. Second, the modernization added a continuing education requirement: individual brokers must complete 36 credits per triennial period and certify completion when filing their triennial status report, with the first certifications tied to the 2026–2027 reporting window. Questions on permits, status reports, and continuing education are exactly the current-regulation material CBP likes to test.

A realistic study plan

Plan for roughly three to four months of consistent study, more if customs is new to you. The most important principle is to study with the actual references, not just a commercial outline. A summary teaches the concept; only working inside the HTSUS and 19 CFR teaches you to find the answer fast.

  • Weeks 1–4: build the map. Read the structure of 19 CFR and the front matter of the HTSUS. Learn what each part of 19 CFR covers so a question's topic instantly tells you which part to open.
  • Weeks 5–10: drill by topic. Take one subject area at a time — entry, classification, valuation, Part 111 — and work practice questions with the references open, tabbing your copies as you go.
  • Weeks 11–14: take full past exams under timed conditions. CBP publishes past exams and answer keys for free. Sit a complete 80-question exam in 4.5 hours, score it, then review every question — especially ones you got right by luck — to see where the answer lived.
  • Final week: refine tabs and speed. Re-tab anything you fumbled and rehearse skipping and flagging.

Aim to take at least four or five complete past exams before test day; most who pass report that their scores climbed steadily as their navigation got faster.

The most common mistakes

  • Treating "open-book" as easy. The book does not help if you cannot find the page; candidates who do not practice with the references run out of time.
  • Memorizing answers from old exams instead of learning the lookup path. CBP rarely reuses exact questions; it tests the same regulations in new fact patterns.
  • Spending too long on classification early. Those questions are the most time-expensive; bank the faster regulatory questions first.
  • Ignoring Section and Chapter Notes in classification, which are precisely where the "obvious" answer gets overturned.
  • Not reading the question carefully. Many items hinge on a single qualifier — "except," "not," a date, or a dollar threshold.
  • Relying on unauthorized materials. Use only what the current Notice of Examination permits.

Exam-day strategy for an open-book test

Time management wins this exam. With about 3.3 minutes per question, set a pace and respect it: on your first pass, answer what you can quickly and flag anything requiring deep classification work. For each question, identify the subject area first, then go straight to the part of the reference where that topic lives — your tabs and practice should make this nearly automatic. Never leave a question blank; there is no penalty for guessing, so use elimination aggressively. Reserve the final 30 minutes to revisit flagged questions and confirm every item has an answer recorded.

After you pass: getting licensed

Passing the exam is a prerequisite for the license, not the license itself. CBP notifies you of your score, and if you pass you must then apply for the broker license, generally within a limited window — historically up to three years from notification. The application is filed through CBP's electronic broker systems (the modern equivalent of CBP Form 3124) and requires:

  • Proof that you meet the basic eligibility requirements (U.S. citizen, at least 21, not a current federal employee).
  • A background investigation, including fingerprints submitted for an FBI criminal-history check.
  • Supporting documentation such as a current credit report and disclosure of your employment and character history.
  • Payment of the required application and processing fees.

CBP reviews your application and background results to confirm you possess the business integrity, good character, and financial responsibility required of a broker. Once approved, you receive your individual customs broker license and become subject to Part 111's ongoing obligations. Always confirm the current steps, forms, and fees on CBP's official website before you apply, since the process is updated periodically.

Frequently asked questions

How hard is the Customs Broker License Exam?

It is genuinely difficult; pass rates are often in the 10 to 20 percent range, though they vary by sitting. The difficulty comes from time pressure and the need to navigate thousands of pages quickly, not from trick questions. Candidates who practice extensively with the actual references pass at much higher rates.

How many times can I take the exam?

There is no limit. You register and pay the fee for each attempt. Many successful brokers passed on a second or third try, using each attempt's published answer key to sharpen their preparation.

Do I need a college degree or prior experience to take the exam?

No. There is no education or work-experience prerequisite to sit for the exam. To obtain the license after passing, you must be a U.S. citizen, at least 21 years old, of good moral character, and not a federal employee.

What can I use during the exam?

Only the reference materials CBP authorizes for that cycle — primarily the HTSUS and Title 19 of the CFR, plus any supplementary documents named in the Notice of Examination. Personal notes and commercial study guides are not permitted, so read the Notice carefully.

Is the exam still open-book if it is computer-based?

Yes. The move to computer-based delivery through Pearson VUE did not change the open-book nature of the exam. You still have access to the official references; the format simply changed how questions are presented and lets you flag and revisit them.

What score do I need to pass, and how is it scored?

You need 75 percent — 60 correct out of 80 questions. There is no penalty for wrong answers, so answer every question even if you must guess.

How soon after passing can I start working as a broker?

You can work in customs-compliance roles immediately, but you cannot independently transact customs business under your own authority until the license is granted. The application, background investigation, and fingerprint check take time, so plan for a gap of several months.

What ongoing requirements come with the license?

Licensed individual brokers must file a triennial status report, pay the fee, and — under the modernized Part 111 — complete and certify 36 continuing-education credits per triennial period, while maintaining required records and meeting Part 111's conduct and supervision standards at all times.
