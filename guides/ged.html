GED Study Guide: The Four Subjects and How to Pass Them

Everything on the GED — the four subject tests, how scoring works, and a practical study plan to earn your high-school-equivalency credential.

▶ Start the free GED practice test

The GED is a route back to opportunity for adults who never finished high school. Earning it tells employers, colleges, and the military that you have the academic skills of a high school graduate, opening doors that a missing diploma keeps shut. But the modern GED is not the test your parents remember. It is fully computer-based, covers four challenging subjects, and is scored on a scale that can even certify you as ready for college-level work. This guide walks you through exactly what the GED measures, how each subject is built and timed, how the scoring really works, and how to put together a study plan that gets you across the finish line without wasting months on the wrong things.

What the GED is and what the credential means

GED stands for General Educational Development. It is a battery of four subject tests that together certify high school equivalency. Passing all four earns you a state-issued high school equivalency credential that is recognized by virtually all U.S. employers and colleges as the legal equivalent of a high school diploma. The test is owned and operated by GED Testing Service, and you register and take official practice tests through the GED.com account portal.

It is worth being clear about what the credential is and is not. It is not a literal high school diploma issued by a school district, but it is treated as equivalent for nearly every practical purpose: job applications, college admissions, financial aid, trade apprenticeships, and military enlistment. Because the test is built to mirror the skills of graduating high school seniors, passing it genuinely demonstrates that you can read complex texts, reason mathematically, interpret data, and write a clear argument.

Who the GED is for

The GED exists for people whose path through traditional school was interrupted. That includes adults who left school years ago, people who were homeschooled and want an official credential, immigrants who completed schooling abroad, and younger people whose circumstances pulled them away from a classroom. You generally cannot hold a high school diploma already, and you must not be currently enrolled in high school.

Age rules are set by each state. Most testers are 18 or older, but in many states 16- and 17-year-olds can test with a signed parental or school waiver. Some states also require a short residency or jurisdiction check. Because these rules vary, confirm your state's specific eligibility and waiver requirements on your GED.com account before you schedule anything.

The four subject tests at a glance

The GED is made of four independent subject tests. You do not have to take them on the same day, and you do not have to take them in any particular order. Each one is scored on its own, and you simply have to reach a passing score on each.

  • Mathematical Reasoning — 115 minutes, with a short no-calculator part and a longer calculator part.
  • Reasoning Through Language Arts (RLA) — about 150 minutes including an essay and a 10-minute break.
  • Science — about 90 minutes.
  • Social Studies — about 70 minutes.

All four are delivered on a computer, whether you sit at an official testing center or take the online proctored version at home. Question formats go well beyond simple multiple choice and include drag-and-drop, fill-in-the-blank, drop-down menus inside passages, and "hot spot" questions where you click a point on an image or chart.

Mathematical Reasoning

Math runs 115 minutes and is split into two parts. Part 1 is a short no-calculator section of about five questions in roughly 12 minutes, testing your raw number sense: arithmetic, fractions, exponents, and roots. Part 2 is the larger calculator-allowed section of about 40 questions, where an on-screen Texas Instruments TI-30XS MultiView calculator is available. Content spans quantitative problem solving (number operations, ratios, percentages, geometry) and algebraic problem solving (expressions, equations, inequalities, functions, and graphs).

An important and reassuring detail: a formula sheet is provided both on screen and in the testing center. You are not expected to memorize the area of a trapezoid or the quadratic formula. The test rewards knowing when and how to apply a formula, not your ability to recall it from memory. Most students find math the hardest of the four subjects, so plan to give it the most study time.

Reasoning Through Language Arts (RLA)

RLA is the longest test at roughly 150 minutes and is built in three parts with a 10-minute break partway through. It combines reading comprehension, language mechanics, and one written essay. Roughly three-quarters of the reading passages are informational (nonfiction, workplace documents, historical texts) and about a quarter are literary. You will answer questions on main ideas, supporting evidence, author's purpose, and tone, plus "language" questions that test grammar, usage, and sentence structure by having you edit drop-down choices inside a passage.

The centerpiece is the Extended Response, a 45-minute analytical essay. You read two passages that argue opposite sides of an issue, then write an essay explaining which argument is better supported by evidence, and why. You are not graded on your personal opinion. You are graded on how well you analyze the arguments, cite specific evidence from the passages, organize your response, and write in clear standard English. A strong essay is typically three to seven paragraphs and 300 to 500 words.

Science

The Science test takes about 90 minutes and draws on three content areas: life science (biology, ecology, the human body), physical science (chemistry and physics basics, energy, motion), and Earth and space science. The good news is that the GED does not expect you to have memorized a textbook. It tests scientific reasoning: reading a passage or experiment, interpreting a graph or data table, identifying a hypothesis, and drawing valid conclusions. Two short-answer items may ask you to write a brief evidence-based response, and there are also calculation-style questions where basic math meets science data.

Social Studies

Social Studies is the shortest test at about 70 minutes. Its weighting leans heavily toward civics and government, which makes up roughly half the questions. The rest covers U.S. history, economics, and geography and the world, in smaller shares. As with Science, the emphasis is on skills: reading historical documents, interpreting political cartoons, analyzing maps and charts, and understanding how government and economics actually work. Knowing how to read a data graphic carefully will earn you more points than memorizing names and dates.

How the scoring works

Each subject is scored on a scale from 100 to 200, and each is independent. There is no averaging across subjects and no overall combined score that can rescue a weak subject. You must reach the passing line on every test on its own.

  • 100–144 — Below passing. You did not pass that subject and will need to retake it.
  • 145–164 — Passing / High School Equivalency. You demonstrated the skills of a graduating high school senior. This is the level you need on all four subjects to earn your credential.
  • 165–174 — College Ready. You showed skills strong enough to start college-level courses, and some colleges will let you skip placement testing or remedial classes.
  • 175–200 — College Ready + Credit. The highest band, which can make you eligible to earn actual college credit at participating institutions, potentially saving you tuition money later.

For most people, the goal is simply 145 on each subject. But if you plan to enroll in college, aiming higher can pay off directly by letting you skip remedial coursework or even bank credit before you start.

Can you take subjects one at a time?

Yes, and for most people that is the smartest approach. The four subjects are scheduled, paid for, and scored separately. You can register for just one test, pass it, and come back for the next whenever you are ready. This lets you knock out your strongest subject first to build momentum and confidence, then concentrate your study time on the harder ones. You only pay for what you register for, which is typically in the range of $30 to $40 per subject depending on your state.

The retake policy is forgiving. If you do not pass, your first and second retakes of a subject usually have no waiting period, and many states discount those retakes when taken at a test center. After a third unsuccessful attempt, most states require a 60-day wait before you try that subject again. Because you only retake the specific subject you failed, a single weak area never forces you to redo work you already passed.

The GED Ready official practice test

The single most useful tool in your preparation is GED Ready, the official practice test sold on GED.com for about $7.99 per subject. It is the only practice test designed to predict your performance on the real exam, because it is calibrated against the actual test's scoring. After you finish, your result lands in one of three color-coded zones: green ("Likely to Pass"), yellow ("Too Close to Call"), and red ("Not Likely to Pass").

Treat GED Ready as a go/no-go signal. A green score is a strong indication you are ready to schedule the real thing. Just as valuable, GED Ready returns a personalized study plan that points you to the exact skills and lessons you got wrong, so you can target your remaining study time instead of reviewing material you have already mastered. In some states, passing GED Ready in a subject is actually required before you are allowed to take that subject in the online proctored format.

A realistic subject-by-subject study plan

A workable plan for most adults is 8 to 12 weeks of steady study, an hour most days, adjusted up or down based on how recently you were in school. Diagnose first: take a free practice quiz in each subject to see where you stand, then weight your schedule toward your weak areas rather than splitting time evenly.

  • Mathematical Reasoning. Budget the most time here. Rebuild fundamentals first: fractions, decimals, percentages, and ratios. Then layer on basic algebra (solving equations, working with expressions and functions) and geometry. Learn the on-screen TI-30XS calculator early so it is not a distraction on test day, and practice reading the provided formula sheet so you can find what you need quickly.
  • RLA. Read challenging nonfiction daily, such as news analysis or opinion essays, and practice identifying the main claim and the evidence behind it. Drill the most common grammar rules (subject-verb agreement, verb tense, punctuation, parallel structure). Write at least three or four full practice essays under a 45-minute timer so the format becomes automatic.
  • Science. Focus on skills over facts. Practice reading data tables and graphs, identifying independent and dependent variables, and writing short evidence-based answers. Review a high-level overview of biology, basic chemistry and physics, and Earth science, but spend your energy on interpreting passages and data.
  • Social Studies. Prioritize civics and government, since it is the largest slice. Understand the branches of government, the Constitution and Bill of Rights, and how elections work. Practice reading primary-source excerpts, maps, and charts. Brush up on basic economics terms like supply, demand, and inflation.

Finish each subject's preparation with a GED Ready test. If you land green, schedule the real exam while the material is fresh.

The most common mistakes

  • Skipping the official practice test. Free practice quizzes are fine for warming up, but only GED Ready reliably predicts whether you will pass. Walking into the real test without it is a gamble.
  • Studying everything evenly. Time spent re-learning skills you already have is time stolen from your weak areas. Diagnose, then concentrate.
  • Memorizing math formulas. They are provided. Spend that effort on knowing which formula a problem calls for and how to plug numbers into it.
  • Treating the essay as an opinion piece. The Extended Response rewards analysis of the passages' evidence, not your personal views. Off-topic personal essays score poorly no matter how passionate.
  • Poor time management. Spending too long on one hard math item or one dense reading passage can leave easy points unanswered at the end. Flag and move on.
  • Leaving answers blank. There is no penalty for guessing, so never leave a question empty.

Extended Response and essay strategy

The RLA essay is the part students fear most, but it follows a predictable formula you can master. With 45 minutes, spend the first 8 to 10 minutes reading both passages and underlining (mentally or on scratch paper) the strongest evidence each side uses. Decide which argument is better supported, not which one you personally agree with. That distinction is the single biggest key to a passing essay.

Use a clear structure: an introduction that states which argument is better supported, two or three body paragraphs that each cite a specific piece of evidence from the passages and explain why it is effective (or why the opposing side's evidence is weak), and a short conclusion. Quote or paraphrase directly from the text. Save three to five minutes at the end to proofread for grammar, spelling, and clear sentences. Graders look for analysis grounded in the passages, logical organization, and standard English, so hitting all three earns you the score you need.

Test-day tips: testing center vs. online proctored

You can take the GED two ways. At an official testing center, you check in with photo ID, store your belongings, and test on the center's computer under in-person supervision. The online proctored option lets you test from home with a live human proctor watching through your webcam, but it has stricter requirements: a quiet, private room with no one else present, a working webcam and microphone, a reliable internet connection, and a clear desk. Some states require a passing GED Ready score in the subject before you can book the online version.

Whichever you choose, prepare the day before. Confirm your ID matches your registration exactly. Sleep well and eat beforehand. For the online test, run the system check and clear your room well ahead of time, because anything that looks like notes, a second screen, or another person can pause or void your session. During the test, use the on-screen flag tool to mark tough questions and return to them, watch the timer, and remember the formula sheet and calculator are always one click away in math. Stay calm: you can retake any single subject if needed, so treat each test as one focused task rather than an all-or-nothing event.

Frequently asked questions

What score do I need to pass the GED?

You need at least 145 on each of the four subjects. Scores range from 100 to 200, and 145 to 164 is the passing/high-school-equivalency band. Because there is no averaging, every subject must independently reach 145.

Do I have to take all four subjects at once?

No. Each subject is scheduled, paid for, and scored separately, so you can take them one at a time in any order. Many people pass their strongest subject first and save the hardest for last.

How long does the whole GED take?

The four tests total roughly seven hours of testing time: about 115 minutes for Math, 150 for RLA, 90 for Science, and 70 for Social Studies. Because you can split them across separate days, you rarely sit for more than one or two at a time.

How much does the GED cost?

Pricing is set by each state and is typically about $30 to $40 per subject, so roughly $120 to $160 to complete all four on the first attempt. The official GED Ready practice test costs about $7.99 per subject on top of that.

Can I use a calculator on the math test?

Yes, on most of it. Math has a short no-calculator section of about five questions, then a longer section where an on-screen TI-30XS MultiView calculator is provided. A formula sheet is also available throughout, so you do not need to memorize formulas.

What is the GED Ready test and is it worth it?

GED Ready is the official practice test that predicts whether you will pass the real exam. It assigns a green, yellow, or red result and gives you a targeted study plan based on your mistakes. It is the most reliable readiness check available and, in some states, is required before you can take a subject online.

What does a "college ready" score mean?

Scoring 165 to 174 on a subject marks you as college ready, which can exempt you from placement tests or remedial courses at some colleges. Scoring 175 to 200 can make you eligible to earn actual college credit, depending on the institution.

How many times can I retake a subject if I fail?

You only retake the specific subject you did not pass. The first and second retakes usually have no waiting period, and many states discount them at test centers. After a third attempt, most states require a 60-day wait before trying that subject again.
