U.S. Citizenship Test: Civics, English, and the Interview

A clear walkthrough of the naturalization process — eligibility, the civics test, the English test, and the interview — with a study plan that works.

▶ Start the free Citizenship practice test

Becoming a U.S. citizen through naturalization is one of the most significant milestones a lawful permanent resident can reach, and the process is far more learnable than it first appears. The exam itself is not a trick. It draws from a published list of questions you can study word for word, the English portion uses simple sentences, and tens of thousands of people pass every month. What trips applicants up is usually preparation gaps and small administrative mistakes, not the difficulty of the material. This guide walks you through eligibility, the N-400 application, biometrics, the interview, and both the civics test and the English test, with an honest study plan and a section on the rules that changed in late 2025. Read it once to understand the whole journey, then come back to the study-plan and civics sections as your test date approaches.

What naturalization is, and who is eligible

Naturalization is the legal process by which a foreign-born person who already holds a green card becomes a U.S. citizen. Citizenship grants the right to vote in federal elections, hold a U.S. passport, sponsor more family members, run for most public offices, and live abroad without losing status. You apply on Form N-400, attend an interview, pass the tests, and take an Oath of Allegiance.

Most applicants qualify under one of two main timelines:

  • The 5-year rule: You have been a lawful permanent resident for at least five years.
  • The 3-year rule: You have been a permanent resident for at least three years, are married to and living with the same U.S. citizen for those three years, and that spouse has been a citizen the whole time.

Beyond the green-card timeline, you generally must meet these core requirements:

  • Age: Be at least 18 years old at the time you file.
  • Continuous residence: Have lived continuously in the U.S. as a permanent resident for the full five (or three) years before filing. Long trips abroad can break this. A trip of six months to one year may disrupt it, and a trip of one year or more usually breaks it unless you took specific steps to preserve residence.
  • Physical presence: Have been physically inside the U.S. for at least 30 months out of the past 5 years (or 18 months out of the past 3 years). Add up your actual days in the country.
  • State or district residence: Have lived for at least three months in the state or USCIS district where you file.
  • Good moral character: Demonstrate good moral character during the statutory period. Certain criminal offenses, failure to pay child support, lying to immigration officials, or not registering for Selective Service (when required) can create problems. Honesty is essential, because issues are often forgivable but concealment rarely is.
  • Attachment to the Constitution: Be willing to support the principles of the U.S. Constitution and take the Oath of Allegiance.

The N-400 application and fees

The N-400 is the application for naturalization. As of 2026 you can file it online or by mail. The standard filing fee is $710 when you file online and $760 by paper, and that amount includes biometrics. A reduced fee of $380 is available to lower-income applicants who qualify, and some very low-income applicants may request a full fee waiver. Always confirm the current fee on the official USCIS N-400 page before you pay, because fees are updated periodically.

The form asks about your travel history, addresses, employers, marital history, children, tax filings, and any encounters with law enforcement. Accuracy matters enormously. Gather your green card, passports, travel records, and tax transcripts before you start, and keep your answers consistent with what immigration already has on file. After USCIS receives and accepts your application, you will get a receipt notice, then a biometrics appointment notice, and eventually an interview notice.

The biometrics appointment

Biometrics is a short, routine step. You visit a USCIS Application Support Center where staff take your fingerprints, photograph, and signature so the agency can run background checks. In many cases USCIS now reuses fingerprints already on file and may waive a separate appointment. Bring your appointment notice and a government-issued photo ID. There is nothing to study here, but do not skip it. Missing biometrics without rescheduling can cause your case to be denied as abandoned.

The naturalization interview

The interview is the heart of the process. A USCIS officer reviews your N-400 with you under oath, confirms that everything is still accurate, and administers the English and civics tests. The officer will ask you to confirm or update facts about your travel, work, family, and background, so reread your own application beforehand. The same conversation also serves as the speaking portion of the English test, because the officer is listening to how well you understand and respond in English throughout.

Treat the interview as a verification meeting rather than an interrogation. Answer truthfully, ask the officer to repeat or rephrase anything you do not understand, and bring updated documents for anything that changed since you filed, such as a new address, a new trip abroad, or a new tax year.

The civics test: an important 2025 change

The civics test checks your knowledge of U.S. history and government. There are now two versions in active use, and which one you take depends on when you filed your N-400.

The 2008 test (filed before October 20, 2025)

If you filed your N-400 before October 20, 2025, you take the long-standing 2008 test. It uses a bank of 100 questions. The officer asks up to 10 of them orally, and you must answer 6 correctly to pass. The officer stops as soon as you reach 6 correct.

The 2025 test (filed on or after October 20, 2025)

If you filed on or after October 20, 2025, you take the new 2025 test. It uses a larger bank of 128 questions. The officer asks up to 20 of them orally, and you must answer 12 correctly to pass. The content is similar in spirit, but the bank is bigger and the bar is higher, so check your filing date carefully and study the correct list. USCIS publishes both lists for free, along with a study guide titled "One Nation, One People" for the 2025 version.

What the questions cover

Both versions group questions into the same broad categories:

  • American government: principles of democracy, the system and branches of government (legislative, executive, judicial), checks and balances, the Constitution, the Bill of Rights, and rights and responsibilities of citizens.
  • American history: the colonial period and independence, the writing of the Constitution, the 1800s including the Civil War, and recent American history from the 1900s onward.
  • Integrated civics: U.S. geography, national symbols such as the flag, and major holidays.

One detail to remember: some answers change over time. Questions about the current President, Vice President, Speaker of the House, your governor, your U.S. senators, your representative, and the Chief Justice all have answers that depend on today's officeholders and on where you live. Learn the current correct answers shortly before your interview rather than memorizing an old answer that may now be wrong, and use the official USCIS "who is" lookups for your state.

The English test: speaking, reading, and writing

The English requirement has three parts, and each is deliberately simple.

  • Speaking: There is no separate speaking quiz. The officer evaluates your ability to speak and understand English by how you answer the normal interview questions about your application.
  • Reading: You will be shown up to three sentences and must read one of three correctly aloud. The vocabulary comes from a published reading word list focused on civics and history terms.
  • Writing: The officer dictates sentences, and you must write one of three correctly. Minor spelling errors are usually acceptable as long as the sentence is understandable. The vocabulary comes from a published writing word list.

USCIS provides the official reading and writing vocabulary lists for free, so you are not guessing about which words might appear. Practicing those exact lists is the most efficient way to prepare for this portion.

Special considerations: age, long residence, and disability

Several rules ease the requirements for older, long-term residents and for people with qualifying disabilities. These do not change the eligibility timelines above. They change how the tests are administered.

  • The 50/20 exception: If you are 50 or older and have been a permanent resident for at least 20 years, you are exempt from the English test and may take the interview and civics test in your native language. You must bring a qualified interpreter.
  • The 55/15 exception: If you are 55 or older and have been a permanent resident for at least 15 years, the same applies. You are exempt from English and may test in your own language with an interpreter.
  • The 65/20 special consideration: If you are 65 or older and have been a permanent resident for at least 20 years, you get both the English exemption and a simplified civics test. You study only a special, shorter list of questions (marked with an asterisk on the official bank), are asked up to 10 of them, and need 6 correct, and you may take it in your native language. Notably, these 65/20 numbers stayed the same even after the 2025 civics changes.
  • The disability exception (Form N-648): If a physical or developmental disability or mental impairment prevents you from learning the material, a licensed medical doctor, osteopathic doctor, or clinical psychologist can complete Form N-648 to request an exception to the English and/or civics requirements. The form must be completed within 180 days before you file. USCIS also provides reasonable accommodations, such as sign-language interpreters or extended time, separate from N-648.

A realistic study plan

The single most effective tactic is to study from the official question bank that matches your filing date, because the interview questions come straight from that list. Here is a plan that works for most people over four to six weeks.

  • Step 1, confirm your version. Check your N-400 filing date. Before October 20, 2025 means the 100-question 2008 test; on or after that date means the 128-question 2025 test. Download the matching official list.
  • Step 2, learn in small batches. Split the bank into groups of about 10 to 15 questions. Aim to master one group per study session rather than skimming all of them at once.
  • Step 3, use audio and flashcards. USCIS offers free MP3 audio and flashcards. Hearing the questions read aloud prepares you for the oral format, since you will hear the questions, not read them, at the interview.
  • Step 4, lock in the "current officials" answers last. Look up today's President, Vice President, your governor, your senators, your representative, and the Chief Justice in the final week, and verify them on the official USCIS lookup pages for your state.
  • Step 5, practice English daily. Read one sentence aloud and write one dictated sentence every day using the official reading and writing word lists. A few minutes a day builds real fluency with the vocabulary.
  • Step 6, take full mock tests. In the final week, have someone quiz you orally the way the officer will, and rehearse answering questions about your own N-400.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Studying the wrong test version. With both the 100-question and 128-question tests now in use, confirm your filing date first. Studying the wrong bank wastes effort.
  • Memorizing outdated officials. Answers about current officeholders change. Verify them right before your interview.
  • Treating it as multiple choice. The civics test is oral. Practice saying answers out loud, not just recognizing them.
  • Inconsistent or untruthful answers. Discrepancies between your N-400 and your interview answers raise red flags. Reread your application and tell the truth, even about uncomfortable topics.
  • Ignoring travel and tax records. Long trips abroad and unfiled taxes affect continuous residence and good moral character. Address these before filing, ideally with a qualified immigration attorney or accredited representative.
  • Skipping appointments. Missing biometrics or the interview without rescheduling can lead to denial.

Interview-day tips

  • Arrive early with your appointment notice, green card, current and expired passports, and any documents for changes since you filed.
  • Dress neatly and silence your phone. Treat it as an important, respectful appointment.
  • Listen carefully and stay calm. If you do not understand a question, politely ask the officer to repeat or rephrase it. That is allowed and shows comprehension.
  • Answer concisely and truthfully. You do not need to volunteer extra information, but never give a false answer.
  • For the civics test, stop when you pass. The officer ends the civics portion once you reach the number of correct answers needed, so steady accuracy matters more than speed.

What happens after you pass

If you pass the tests and the officer is satisfied with your eligibility, your case can be approved. Sometimes you receive a decision the same day, and sometimes USCIS needs more time or more documents. Once approved, you are scheduled for an Oath of Allegiance ceremony, which is the final legal step. In some locations you can take the oath the same day; in others you receive a later date. At the ceremony you return your green card, take the oath, and receive your Certificate of Naturalization. From that moment you are a U.S. citizen. Soon after, you can apply for a U.S. passport and register to vote.

Frequently asked questions

How many questions are on the civics test and how many do I need right?

It depends on when you filed Form N-400. If you filed before October 20, 2025, you take the 2008 test: the officer asks up to 10 questions from a 100-question bank and you need 6 correct. If you filed on or after October 20, 2025, you take the 2025 test: the officer asks up to 20 questions from a 128-question bank and you need 12 correct.

Is the civics test multiple choice?

No. For most applicants it is an oral test. The officer reads questions aloud and you answer out loud, so practice speaking your answers rather than only reading them.

Do I have to take the test in English?

Usually yes, but there are exceptions. Under the 50/20 and 55/15 rules, qualifying older long-term residents are exempt from English and may test in their native language with an interpreter. Under the 65/20 rule, you also get a simplified civics list in your native language. A disability exception (Form N-648) can waive English and/or civics for those who medically qualify.

What is the English test like?

It has three parts. Speaking is assessed naturally as you answer interview questions. For reading, you must read one of up to three sentences correctly. For writing, you must write one of up to three dictated sentences correctly. USCIS publishes the exact reading and writing vocabulary lists for free.

What if I fail part of the test?

If you fail the English or civics portion at your first interview, USCIS gives you a second chance. You are generally rescheduled to retake only the part you failed, usually within 60 to 90 days. If you fail again, your application is typically denied, but you can reapply later.

How much does it cost and how long does it take?

As of 2026 the N-400 fee is $710 online or $760 by paper, including biometrics, with a reduced $380 fee for qualifying low-income applicants. Always confirm the current amount with USCIS. Total processing time varies widely by field office, often several months to over a year from filing to oath.

Why do some answers, like the President's name, keep changing?

Several civics questions ask about current officeholders, such as the President, Vice President, Speaker of the House, your governor, your U.S. senators, your representative, and the Chief Justice. Those answers change with elections and appointments and vary by where you live, so learn the current correct answers shortly before your interview using the official USCIS lookups.

Can I apply for citizenship before my five years are up?

You may file your N-400 up to 90 days before you reach the five-year (or three-year) mark, but you must still meet all continuous-residence and physical-presence requirements as of your filing date. Filing earlier than the 90-day window can lead to a rejection, so count carefully.
