Food Handler Card: The Complete Food Safety Study Guide

Pass your food handler exam with confidence — temperatures, hygiene, cross-contamination, allergens, and the food-safety rules that show up on every test.

▶ Start the free Food Handler practice test

If you work anywhere food is prepared, served, or sold, there is a good chance you need a food handler card before your first shift. The good news: it is one of the most achievable credentials in the working world. Most food handler exams are short, multiple-choice, often open-book, and built around a small set of practical rules you will use every day. This guide walks you through exactly what a food handler card is, who needs one, how the exam works, and the core food-safety knowledge that shows up on virtually every version of the test. Read it once carefully, take a few practice quizzes, and you will walk into the exam confident rather than guessing.

What a food handler card actually is

A food handler card (sometimes called a food handler permit, food worker card, or food safety certificate) is an entry-level credential proving that a food employee understands basic food-safety practices. It is aimed at the people on the front line of food service: line cooks, prep cooks, servers, bussers, dishwashers, baristas, deli and grocery staff, bartenders, food-truck workers, and concession employees. The training covers personal hygiene, the temperature danger zone, cross-contamination, allergens, and cleaning and sanitizing—the everyday habits that prevent people from getting sick.

The card is issued after you complete a short training course and pass a final exam. Many approved programs are ANSI National Accreditation Board (ANAB) accredited, meaning the course and the organization behind it have passed a rigorous third-party review against national standards. When your state or county requires an "accredited" program, it generally means ANAB-accredited. Cards are inexpensive (often under $15), can usually be finished in a couple of hours online, and typically stay valid for about three years—though some jurisdictions renew sooner, so always check your local rule.

Food handler card vs. manager certification

This is the single most common point of confusion, so be clear on it. A food handler card is for employees. A Food Protection Manager Certification—the best-known example is the ServSafe Manager credential—is a higher-level qualification for the person in charge of food safety at an establishment. The two are not interchangeable:

  • Food handler card: entry-level, short course, basic concepts, often open-book and online, lower passing threshold, valid roughly three years.
  • Manager certification (e.g., ServSafe Manager): a proctored exam, deeper material on HACCP, regulatory compliance, active managerial control, and supervising staff. It is required in many states or counties for at least one person on site, often called the "Person in Charge."

In short: nearly every food worker may need a food handler card, but a kitchen typically needs only one (or a few) certified managers. Having a manager certification usually satisfies the handler requirement, but a handler card does not substitute for a manager certification.

Who needs one—and why requirements vary

Food safety is regulated at the state and local level, layered on top of the FDA's model Food Code (the federal recommendation that most jurisdictions adopt in some form). Because of that, requirements differ a great deal by location:

  • Some states require a food handler card statewide (California, Texas, Arizona, Illinois, Utah, Washington, Oregon, and others have well-known programs).
  • In other states the rule is set county by county or city by city.
  • A few states have no statewide handler requirement at all, but individual employers or local health departments may still require one.

The practical takeaway: confirm the rule for the specific county and city where you will work, not just the state. Ask your employer or check your local health department's website. Also confirm which program they accept—some areas mandate a specific state-run course, while others accept any ANAB-accredited provider.

The exam format

Food handler exams are designed to be passable, not to trip you up. While details vary by provider and state, the typical pattern looks like this:

  • Format: multiple-choice, commonly around 40 questions (some programs use 30–45).
  • Passing score: usually about 70–75%, though some providers set it at 80%. At a 70% threshold on a 40-question exam, you need 28 correct.
  • Delivery: most courses are online and self-paced, and the final exam is frequently open-book and untimed or generously timed.
  • Retakes: many programs give you two attempts (sometimes unlimited) at no extra cost.
  • Certificate: you usually receive a printable certificate immediately on passing.

Because the format is forgiving, the goal is not to memorize everything perfectly—it is to genuinely understand the handful of rules below so you answer quickly and apply them on the job.

Core topic 1: Personal hygiene and handwashing

Food workers are themselves the most common source of contamination, so hygiene is heavily tested. Proper handwashing uses warm running water, soap, and at least 10–15 seconds of scrubbing (about 20 seconds of total handwashing), followed by rinsing and drying with a single-use towel or air dryer. Wash hands before starting work, after using the restroom, after touching your face or hair, after handling raw meat, after taking out trash, after touching dirty dishes, and any time hands may have become contaminated.

Other key rules: gloves are not a substitute for handwashing—wash before putting them on and change them when they tear or become soiled. Use gloves, tongs, or utensils for ready-to-eat foods so you never touch them with bare hands. Keep fingernails short and clean, avoid wearing jewelry beyond a plain band, tie back hair and use a restraint, and never eat, drink, smoke, or chew gum in food-prep areas.

Core topic 2: The temperature danger zone and TCS foods

This is the heart of the exam. Bacteria multiply fastest in the temperature danger zone of 41°F to 135°F. Under ideal conditions some pathogens can double roughly every 20 minutes. The goal of nearly every temperature rule is to keep food out of this zone or to limit its time inside it.

TCS foods—Time/Temperature Control for Safety foods—are the ones that support rapid bacterial growth and therefore need tight temperature control. Examples include meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, cooked rice and pasta, cut leafy greens, cut tomatoes, cut melons, sprouts, and tofu. Memorize the idea that moist, protein-rich, low-acid foods are usually TCS.

The 2-hour / 4-hour rule

If TCS food sits in the danger zone, the clock is running. A widely taught version: discard TCS food left in the danger zone for more than 4 hours total. Many programs teach the stricter "2-hour" guideline for consumers and for some local codes. Know both, and on the job follow whichever your establishment uses. The principle that matters: cumulative time in the danger zone is what counts, and once exceeded the food must be thrown out—you cannot fix it by reheating.

Core topic 3: Cooking, holding, cooling, and reheating temperatures

Use a clean, calibrated thermometer inserted into the thickest part of the food. Common minimum internal cooking temperatures taught for food-service exams are:

  • 165°F — poultry, stuffed foods, and dishes combining cooked ingredients (also the target for reheating and microwaving).
  • 155°F — ground meats (beef, pork), ground seafood, and shell eggs cooked for later service.
  • 145°F — whole cuts of beef, pork, fish/seafood, and eggs cooked to order for immediate service (with the appropriate rest time for roasts).
  • 135°F — fruits, vegetables, grains, and legumes that will be hot-held.

(Note: USDA consumer guidance lists 160°F for ground beef; food-service codes commonly teach 155°F. Follow the value your program uses.)

Holding: keep hot TCS food at 135°F or above and cold TCS food at 41°F or below. Reheating for hot holding must reach 165°F within two hours. Cooling uses a two-stage process: cool from 135°F to 70°F within 2 hours, then from 70°F to 41°F within 4 more hours, for a total of 6 hours. Speed cooling with shallow pans, ice baths, ice paddles, or by cutting large items into smaller portions.

Core topic 4: The Big 6 pathogens and reporting illness

The FDA Food Code highlights six especially contagious pathogens, often called the "Big 6," that can spread from a sick worker to many customers through ready-to-eat food. They are:

  • Norovirus (the most common cause of foodborne illness in the U.S.)
  • Hepatitis A virus
  • Salmonella Typhi (typhoid fever)
  • Nontyphoidal Salmonella
  • Shigella species
  • Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC)

You must report to your manager—and may be restricted or excluded from work—if you are diagnosed with any of these, or if you have certain symptoms: vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, a sore throat with fever, or an infected open wound or lesion with pus. Reporting illness protects the public and is a frequent exam topic, so know these symptoms and pathogens by name.

Core topic 5: Cross-contamination and allergens

Cross-contamination is the transfer of harmful microbes from one surface or food to another—classically from raw meat to ready-to-eat food via hands, cutting boards, knives, or splashing. Prevent it by separating raw and ready-to-eat foods, using color-coded cutting boards and dedicated utensils, washing and sanitizing surfaces between tasks, and storing foods correctly (see FIFO below).

The major food allergens

Allergic reactions can be life-threatening, so allergen awareness is tested. The U.S. recognizes nine major allergens (the "Big 9"): milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, and sesame (added as the ninth in 2023). For allergen safety, take orders accurately, avoid cross-contact (using clean equipment, gloves, and surfaces for allergen orders), and know how to communicate ingredients honestly to guests. Cross-contact is not the same as cross-contamination, and cooking does not destroy an allergen the way it kills bacteria.

Core topic 6: Cleaning vs. sanitizing

These two words are not synonyms, and the exam wants you to know the difference. Cleaning removes visible dirt, grease, and food debris using soap or detergent and water. Sanitizing reduces pathogens on an already-clean surface to safe levels, using heat or an approved chemical sanitizer (such as chlorine, quaternary ammonium, or iodine) at the correct concentration and contact time. The correct order is always clean first, then sanitize—you cannot sanitize a dirty surface effectively. For manual warewashing, remember the standard three-compartment sink sequence: wash, rinse, sanitize, then air dry (never towel dry).

Core topic 7: Receiving, storage order, thawing, and FIFO

FIFO—First In, First Out—means using older stock before newer stock by placing newly received items behind existing ones and labeling with prep or use-by dates. It reduces waste and prevents serving expired food.

Storage order in the cooler

Store foods top-to-bottom by their minimum cooking temperature, so anything that drips lands on food that will be cooked hotter (or already-cooked food stays protected):

  • Top shelf: ready-to-eat foods.
  • Seafood (145°F).
  • Whole cuts of beef and pork (145°F).
  • Ground meats (155°F).
  • Bottom shelf: poultry (165°F).

Receiving and thawing

When receiving deliveries, reject TCS food that arrives in the danger zone, frozen food with signs of thawing or large ice crystals, dented or swollen cans, or items with broken packaging or an off smell. Thaw food safely using one of four approved methods: in the refrigerator (safest), under cold running water (70°F or below), in the microwave if cooked immediately, or as part of the cooking process. Never thaw TCS food at room temperature on the counter.

A realistic study plan

You can be ready in a few short sessions:

  • Session 1 (45–60 min): read your course material or this guide and write down the key numbers—41°F/135°F, 165/155/145/135, the 2-hour cooling and 4-hour discard rules.
  • Session 2 (30–45 min): memorize the Big 6, the nine allergens, the reportable symptoms, and the cooler storage order. Quiz yourself with flashcards.
  • Session 3 (30 min): take a full practice test, then review every question you missed and reread that topic.

If your exam is open-book, build a one-page cheat sheet of the temperatures and lists so you can look up anything quickly. Even open-book, understanding beats hunting—you will finish faster and score higher.

The most common mistakes

  • Confusing the handler card with manager certification—and assuming one covers the other.
  • Mixing up cooking temperatures, especially poultry (165°F) versus ground meat (155°F) versus whole cuts (145°F).
  • Thinking gloves replace handwashing. They do not.
  • Confusing cleaning with sanitizing, or forgetting that you must clean first.
  • Mixing up cross-contamination (microbes) with cross-contact (allergens).
  • Getting the cooler storage order backward—poultry goes on the bottom.
  • Forgetting that reheating cannot rescue food that has been in the danger zone too long.

Test-day and online-exam tips

  • Read each question fully—watch for words like "not," "except," "first," and "minimum," which change the answer.
  • Eliminate obviously wrong choices, then pick the safest food-safety practice; the most cautious answer is usually correct.
  • If the exam is open-book, keep your cheat sheet of temperatures and lists within reach, but answer from understanding first to save time.
  • Use a stable internet connection and a real computer if possible for online exams; have your ID and payment ready if a certificate fee applies.
  • Don't overthink. These exams reward common-sense safety, not trick logic.
  • If you fail, note your weak topics, restudy just those, and use your free retake—most programs allow it.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to get a food handler card?

Most online courses take about one to two hours to complete, including the exam. Many people finish the whole thing—training, test, and printable certificate—in a single sitting.

What is the passing score on the food handler exam?

It varies by provider and state, but a common threshold is around 70–75%. Some programs require 80%. On a 40-question test with a 70% bar, you need 28 correct answers.

Is the food handler exam open-book?

Often, yes. Many online, self-paced courses allow you to reference the material during the final exam, and most give you more than one attempt. Always confirm with your specific provider, since rules differ.

How is a food handler card different from ServSafe Manager?

A food handler card is an entry-level credential for employees and covers basic practices. ServSafe Manager is a higher-level, proctored certification for the person in charge of food safety and covers deeper material like HACCP and regulatory compliance. A manager cert usually satisfies the handler requirement, but not the reverse.

How long is a food handler card valid?

Typically about three years, but some states and counties require renewal sooner. Check the rule for your specific location and renew before it expires.

Does my food handler card work in every state?

Not necessarily. Requirements are set by state and local health departments, and some jurisdictions only accept a specific state-run program or require their own card. If you move or take a job in a new county, verify that your card is accepted there.

What temperatures do I really need to memorize?

The essentials: the danger zone (41°F–135°F); poultry 165°F; ground meat 155°F; whole cuts and seafood 145°F; hot-held vegetables and grains 135°F; reheating to 165°F within two hours; and the cooling rule of 135°F to 70°F in 2 hours, then to 41°F within 6 hours total.

Do I still need a card if I only serve drinks or bus tables?

Often yes. Many jurisdictions require a card for anyone who handles food, drinks, utensils, or food-contact surfaces—including servers, bartenders, baristas, and bussers. When in doubt, ask your employer or local health department.
