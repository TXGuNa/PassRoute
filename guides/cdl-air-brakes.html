CDL Air Brakes Test: The Complete Guide

How air brake systems work and exactly what the CDL Air Brakes test expects — the parts, the key pressures, the in-cab check, and the mistakes that fail people.

▶ Start the free CDL Air Brakes practice test

Almost every tractor-trailer, straight truck, and motorcoach on the road stops with air-actuated brakes, which is exactly why the CDL Air Brakes test exists. It is a knowledge exam that proves you understand how a compressed-air braking system works, how to inspect it, and how to react when something goes wrong at highway speed. Unlike most endorsements, air brakes are governed by a restriction rather than an endorsement code, and skipping the test has a permanent, printed consequence on your license. This guide covers who needs it, how the system works, the numbers you must memorize, the in-cab check examiners expect, the mistakes that sink test-takers, and a focused study plan to pass the first time.

Who needs the air brakes test (and the "L" restriction)

There is no air-brakes "endorsement" in the usual sense. Instead, federal rules treat air brakes as a knowledge requirement built into the licensing process. If you want to drive any commercial vehicle equipped with a full air-brake system, an air-over-hydraulic system, or air-actuated parking brakes, you must pass the Air Brakes written knowledge test and demonstrate air-brake competency during your skills exam.

Here is the catch that surprises new drivers. If you do not pass the air-brakes knowledge test, or if you take your skills test in a vehicle that does not have air brakes, the state stamps an "L" restriction on your CDL. That restriction legally bars you from operating any CMV equipped with air brakes. Since the overwhelming majority of Class A tractor-trailers and Class B straight trucks and buses use air brakes, an L restriction makes a CDL nearly unusable for serious commercial work.

  • To avoid the L restriction you must (1) pass the written air-brakes knowledge test, (2) pass an air-brake-system inspection during the pre-trip portion of the skills test, and (3) take the driving test in a vehicle that actually has air brakes.
  • Fail or skip any of those three and the restriction applies until you re-test in a properly equipped vehicle.

The test itself is typically around 25 multiple-choice questions, and most states require about 80% correct to pass. The questions are drawn directly from the air-brakes section of your state's CDL manual, so the manual is your single best source of truth.

How an air brake system works, in plain terms

An air brake system uses compressed air the way a hydraulic system uses fluid: as the force that pushes the brakes against the wheels. Because air is endlessly available, the system can be built with large reserves and fail-safe parking brakes. Three sub-systems work together: one that makes and stores air, one that delivers air to the brakes when you press the pedal, and the mechanical foundation brakes that do the actual stopping.

Making and storing the air

  • Air compressor — driven by the engine, it pumps air into the storage tanks. It may be connected to its own oil supply or share the engine oil.
  • Governor — controls when the compressor pumps air into the tanks and when it stops, keeping pressure inside a working band rather than letting it climb forever.
  • Air reservoirs (tanks) — store the compressed air so you always have a reserve. They hold enough air for several brake applications even if the compressor stops.
  • Air dryer / alcohol evaporator — removes water and oil from the air. Because compressing air creates moisture, tanks have drains that must be bled daily to prevent water (which can freeze and disable the brakes) from collecting.

Delivering the air and stopping the wheels

  • Foundation brakes — at each wheel, most commonly the s-cam drum type. When you brake, air pushes a rod that rotates an s-shaped cam, forcing the brake shoes against the drum.
  • Brake chambers — convert air pressure into mechanical push. Air enters the chamber, pushes a diaphragm, and drives the push rod outward.
  • Slack adjusters — the arm that connects the push rod to the s-cam. As linings wear, the stroke gets longer; slack adjusters keep that stroke in spec. With the brakes applied, the push rod should not move more than about an inch where it leaves the chamber; excessive travel means the brakes are out of adjustment, the single most common out-of-service violation.

The dual air brake system

Modern heavy trucks use a dual air brake system: two completely separate air systems sharing one set of controls. One system typically operates the rear axle brakes and the other the front, so if one circuit fails you still have brakes on part of the vehicle. Each system has its own tanks, lines, and gauges (or a split gauge). Before driving, you build pressure to governor cut-out in both systems, and the low-air warning should activate before pressure in either drops too far.

The parts test-takers must know cold

Supply system vs. service system

The supply (or emergency) side keeps the system charged and the spring brakes held off. The service side is what you operate with the foot pedal to slow the truck. On a combination vehicle these map to two separate trailer air lines, and confusing them is a classic test trap.

Tractor protection valve and the trailer air lines

  • Service air line (control line) — carries air controlled by the foot brake or trailer hand valve to the trailer's service brakes. Air flows only when you brake.
  • Emergency air line (supply line) — charges the trailer reservoirs and controls the trailer emergency brakes. It is pressurized whenever the system is charged.
  • Tractor protection valve — closes the air supply between tractor and trailer if air pressure drops dangerously low (roughly 20 to 45 psi). It keeps air in the tractor so the driver still has braking, and it automatically applies the trailer's emergency brakes if the trailer breaks away.
  • Trailer air supply valve — a red eight-sided knob in the cab the driver uses to open and close the supply line to the trailer.

Spring brakes and parking brakes

Heavy vehicles use powerful springs for parking and emergency braking. When the system is charged, air pressure holds the springs back (off). When you set the parking brake, or when air pressure is lost, the springs release and apply the brakes mechanically. This is why a major air leak will eventually bring the truck to a hard stop on its own. On a typical tractor, the spring brakes fully apply somewhere in the 20 to 45 psi range. Never push the brake pedal down when the spring brakes are on; combining air application and spring force can damage the brakes.

Key numbers and rules to memorize

The air-brakes test is unusually number-heavy. Commit these to memory, because at least a handful of questions will quote them directly.

  • Low-air warning — must activate before pressure drops below about 60 psi (the standard manual figure; older or specialty vehicles may warn anywhere from 55 to 75 psi, or half the cut-out pressure).
  • Governor cut-out — the compressor stops pumping at roughly 120 to 135 psi (commonly cited as ~125 psi).
  • Governor cut-in — the compressor resumes pumping at about 100 psi.
  • Safety relief valve — set to open at about 150 psi to protect the first tank from over-pressure.
  • Spring brakes apply — fully engage in the 20 to 45 psi range as air is lost.
  • Pressure build-up — in a dual system, air should build from about 85 to 100 psi within roughly 45 seconds.
  • Push-rod stroke — should not exceed about 1 inch at the brake chamber with brakes applied.

The air-loss leakage limits

With the engine off and the system fully charged, the maximum allowed air-pressure loss is:

  • Brakes released: no more than 2 psi per minute (single vehicle) or 3 psi per minute (combination vehicle).
  • Brakes applied (foot brake held down): no more than 3 psi per minute (single vehicle) or 4 psi per minute (combination vehicle).

A handy memory hook: each number goes up by one when you go from single to combination, and up by one again when you apply the brakes. So the sequence is 2, 3, 3, 4.

Inspecting air brakes: pre-trip and the in-cab check

On the skills test you will both verbally inspect air-brake components and perform the in-cab functional check, often described as a three-step (or seven-step) air-brake check. The engine stays off for the leakage and warning-light portions so you test the stored reserve, not the running compressor.

Walk-around air-brake items

  • Check the air compressor drive belt (if belt-driven) for condition and tension.
  • Inspect slack adjusters, brake chambers, and push rods; nothing should be loose, cracked, or leaking, and the push-rod stroke should be in spec.
  • Look at the brake drums/discs and linings; linings should not be dangerously thin, soaked with oil or grease, or cracked.
  • Inspect air lines and hoses for cuts, kinks, and worn spots; on a combination, verify the glad hands are sealed and the service/emergency lines are connected correctly.

The in-cab air-brake check

  • Governor cut-out and cut-in: with the engine running, build pressure to cut-out and confirm the compressor stops; fan the brakes to drop pressure and confirm cut-in.
  • Air loss rate (leak test): charge the system, shut the engine off, release the parking brake, and time the pressure drop for one minute (brakes released), then fully apply and hold the foot brake for another minute (brakes applied). Compare against the 2/3/3/4 limits.
  • Low-air warning: with the engine off, fan the brakes to bleed air and confirm the warning light and/or buzzer comes on at or before about 60 psi.
  • Spring brake activation: keep fanning the brakes; the parking-brake valve should pop out (spring brakes apply) in the 20 to 45 psi range.
  • Build-up rate: start the engine, run at fast idle, and confirm the system builds from 85 to 100 psi within about 45 seconds.
  • Parking and service brake tests: at idle, set the parking brake and gently tug forward to confirm it holds; then release it, roll forward at a few mph, and press the brake to confirm the service brakes stop the vehicle and the truck does not pull to one side.

Common test mistakes

  • Mixing up the leakage numbers. Memorize 2/3/3/4 with the "single vs. combination, released vs. applied" logic rather than as random figures.
  • Confusing cut-out with cut-in. Cut-out is the high number (~125 psi, compressor stops); cut-in is the low number (~100 psi, compressor restarts).
  • Thinking the warning light is just for show. A low-air warning means stop safely and soon; spring brakes can apply unexpectedly if pressure keeps falling.
  • Pumping the brakes to make air last. Repeated stabs at the pedal release air with each application and can drain the tanks faster, leaving you with nothing.
  • Forgetting to drain the tanks. Water in the tanks can freeze and disable the brakes; manual drains must be bled regularly.
  • Running the engine during the leak test. The leak and warning tests must be done with the engine off so you are testing reserves, not the compressor masking a leak.
  • Ignoring stopping-distance and brake-fade concepts. The test covers why brakes fade on long downgrades and why you use a low gear plus light, steady braking rather than riding the pedal.

A focused study plan

  • Day 1 — Read the manual section. Read your state's air-brakes chapter cover to cover once without trying to memorize. Get the big picture: make air, store air, deliver air, stop the wheels.
  • Day 2 — Master the parts. Make flashcards for compressor, governor, reservoirs, foundation brakes, brake chambers, slack adjusters, tractor protection valve, and the two trailer air lines. Be able to explain each in one sentence.
  • Day 3 — Drill the numbers. Memorize 60 psi warning, ~125/~100 cut-out/cut-in, 150 psi safety valve, 20 to 45 psi spring brakes, 45-second build-up, and the 2/3/3/4 leakage limits.
  • Day 4 — Learn the in-cab check as a story. Practice reciting the sequence out loud so it becomes a routine, not a list. This doubles as prep for your skills test.
  • Day 5 — Take practice tests. Work through full-length practice exams until you consistently score above 90%. Review every wrong answer and trace it back to the manual.
  • Throughout: focus extra time on whichever cluster you keep missing, usually the numbers or the service-vs-emergency line distinction.

Frequently asked questions

Is "air brakes" an endorsement or a restriction?

It functions as a restriction, not an endorsement. If you pass the air-brakes knowledge and skills requirements, your license is unrestricted for air brakes. If you do not, the state adds an "L" restriction that prohibits you from driving any CMV with air brakes.

What is the L restriction and how do I get rid of it?

The L restriction bars you from operating air-brake-equipped commercial vehicles. To remove it, return to the DMV, pass the air-brakes knowledge test, and complete the pre-trip and driving portions of the skills test in a vehicle that actually has air brakes.

How many questions are on the air brakes test and what score do I need?

Most states use roughly 25 multiple-choice questions and require about 80% correct to pass. Check your specific state, since the exact count and passing threshold can vary slightly.

What does the low-air warning mean and when does it come on?

It is a light, buzzer, or both that warns the system is losing pressure. Standard equipment must warn before pressure falls below about 60 psi. When it activates, get off the road and stop safely as soon as you can, because spring brakes may apply if pressure keeps dropping.

What are the maximum air-loss leakage rates?

With the engine off and the system charged: with brakes released, no more than 2 psi per minute for a single vehicle and 3 psi for a combination; with the foot brake fully applied, no more than 3 psi for a single vehicle and 4 psi for a combination. Remember the pattern 2, 3, 3, 4.

Why must the engine be off during the leak test?

A running engine keeps the compressor feeding air into the tanks, which can hide a leak by replacing the air as fast as it escapes. Shutting the engine off lets you measure the true loss from the stored reserve.

What happens if I lose all my air pressure while driving?

As pressure falls into the roughly 20 to 45 psi range, the spring brakes apply automatically and the truck comes to a hard stop on its own. That is a fail-safe, not a substitute for monitoring your gauges, so always respond to the low-air warning long before it gets that far.
