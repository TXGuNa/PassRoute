CDL Pre-Trip Inspection: The Complete Guide

Exactly how the CDL pre-trip inspection works — every area you check, what to say out loud, and a method for memorizing the long list.

▶ Start the free CDL Pre-Trip Inspection practice test

The pre-trip inspection is the part of the CDL skills test that surprises the most people. After weeks of studying for the multiple-choice exams, candidates walk up to the truck expecting to do something, and instead discover that the pre-trip is essentially a memorized oral exam. You point at parts, you name them, and you say out loud what you are looking for. The examiner stands beside you with a clipboard and a checklist, marking off whether you covered each required item and whether you said enough about it to prove you actually understand why it matters. This guide breaks down exactly how the test works, what you are expected to say at each station, how to memorize a list that runs well over a hundred items, and the mistakes that quietly fail people who otherwise know how to drive a truck.

What the pre-trip inspection test actually is

The pre-trip is one of three parts of the CDL skills test, the other two being the basic control skills (backing, offset, parallel park) and the on-road drive. Before any wheels turn, you have to demonstrate that you can identify whether a commercial vehicle is safe to operate. You are not repairing anything and you are not running diagnostics. You are walking a fixed route around the truck, touching or pointing at components, and explaining what a defect would look like.

The whole thing takes most candidates 20 to 30 minutes. The examiner is checking three things at once: that you visited every required area, that you named the parts correctly, and that you described what you are inspecting for. That last part is where points are won and lost. Pointing at a tire and saying "tire" earns nothing. Pointing at a tire and saying "the tire is properly inflated, the tread depth is adequate, there are no cuts, bulges, or uneven wear, and the valve stem is intact with a cap" is a full-credit item.

How it is scored

Scoring is item-by-item against a standard inspection sheet. The widely used rule of thumb is to say at least two things about every component, and to physically touch or point to each item so the examiner can see you inspecting it rather than reciting from a parking lot ten feet away. The examiner tallies the items you correctly cover. On most state versions you are allowed a limited number of missed items, but several categories are weighted heavily, and a handful are effectively automatic failures regardless of the rest of your score.

The single most important non-negotiable is the in-cab air brake check. If you skip a step, do the steps out of order, or perform it incorrectly, many states treat it as an automatic failure even if everything else was flawless. Treat the air brake check as a fixed script that must be performed exactly as taught.

How it differs from the written tests

The knowledge (written) tests are multiple choice. You can guess, you get partial recall credit, and nobody watches your hands. The pre-trip is open-ended, from memory, and performed live. There is no word bank prompting you. You either remember that the truck has a power steering pump, a water pump, an alternator, and an air compressor mounted in the engine compartment, or you do not. Where the written test rewards recognition, the pre-trip rewards recall plus performance. That difference is why people who ace the written exams still fail here: the skill being tested is fundamentally different.

The major inspection areas

Most schools teach a seven-block route: vehicle overview/approach, engine compartment, in-cab checks, lights, driver-side walkaround, rear of vehicle, and the air brake check. The exact order varies slightly by state, but the content is consistent.

Engine compartment

With the hood up, you cover fluids, the drive components, and the front steering. Say each fluid is "at the proper level and not leaking." Specifically:

  • Fluids: engine oil, coolant in the reservoir, power steering fluid, and (where present) windshield washer fluid and automatic transmission fluid — proper level, no leaks.
  • Belts: the power steering, water pump, and alternator belts should be snug with roughly half to three-quarters of an inch of play, not frayed, cracked, or glazed. If a belt is not visible, say it is enclosed and you'd check it for proper tension.
  • Hoses: "secure, properly connected, no cracks, cuts, bulges, abrasions, or leaks."
  • Components: water pump, alternator, air compressor, and power steering pump — "securely mounted, not leaking, belt/gear driven as appropriate."
  • Steering: point to the steering box (securely mounted, no leaks, mounting bolts present), and the steering linkage — the pitman arm, drag link, and tie rod — checking that connecting parts are not worn, bent, or cracked, and that cotter pins and castle nuts are present. Missing cotter pins are a classic failure.
  • Front suspension: springs, spring hangers, shackles, and shock absorbers — secure, not cracked, broken, or shifted.

In-cab checks and the air brake check

Inside the cab you start the engine and read the gauges (oil pressure building to normal, air pressure building, ammeter/voltmeter, temperature). You check the safety and emergency equipment: a fire extinguisher that is charged and secured, three reflective triangles or warning devices, and spare electrical fuses unless the vehicle uses circuit breakers. You confirm the windshield is clean and undamaged, the wipers and washers work, the mirrors are clean and adjusted, the horn (city and air) sounds, the heater/defroster works, the seatbelt latches and is not frayed, and the steering wheel has no more than the allowed play (about two inches on a 20-inch wheel).

Then comes the air brake check, the heart of the test. A common script runs:

  • Governor / build-up: with air pressure built to full, the system should be at normal operating range; on tractors the governor cuts out around 120–135 psi.
  • Static leak (applied) test: chock the wheels, release the brakes, shut the engine off with the key on. With the brake pedal pressed and held, air loss should not exceed 3 psi in one minute for a straight truck or 4 psi for a combination vehicle.
  • Low-air warning: with the key on, fan the brake pedal down. The low-air warning light and/or buzzer must come on at or before 60 psi.
  • Spring brake pop-out: keep fanning. The parking brake (and the trailer supply valve on combinations) must pop out, applying the spring brakes, somewhere between 20 and 45 psi.
  • Service brake test: later, after rebuilding air, you roll forward at about 5 mph and apply the brakes firmly — the truck should stop straight with no pulling, grabbing, or delay.

Perform these steps in the order your examiner specifies. Verbalize each one. This is the section examiners watch most closely.

Lights and reflectors

Before walking around, you typically turn on the headlights (low and high beam), four-way flashers, and then later the turn signals and brake lights, and walk the perimeter confirming each lamp works, is the correct color, is clean, and is not cracked. Required colors: amber on front and side markers, red at the rear, white headlights, and red brake/tail lights. Reflectors and reflective tape should be present, clean, and the right color for their position.

The walkaround

This is the longest portion. Working around the truck, you inspect at each wheel and along the frame:

  • Tires: properly inflated (and you can say you'd check with a gauge, not by kicking), adequate tread (4/32" on steer tires, 2/32" on others), no cuts, bulges, or sidewall damage, valve stem and cap intact, and no tires touching on duals.
  • Rims and wheels: no cracks, no welding repairs, no bent rims.
  • Lug nuts: all present, not loose, no rust streaks or shiny threads indicating movement.
  • Hub/oil seals: not leaking, oil at proper level if there's a sight glass.
  • Brakes: brake drum not cracked, brake linings/shoes not worn dangerously thin or contaminated with oil, brake chamber securely mounted, slack adjuster and pushrod with no more than about one inch of free play when pulled by hand.
  • Suspension: springs, hangers, U-bolts, shock absorbers, and torque/radius rods — not cracked, broken, shifted, or missing.
  • Frame: no cracks, no bends, no missing or loose cross members.

Coupling system (combination vehicles)

For a Class A combination, the coupling between tractor and trailer is its own scored block. You point to and describe:

  • Fifth wheel: mounting bolts and brackets secure, no cracks, properly greased; the locking jaws fully closed around the shank (not the head) of the kingpin; the release arm in the locked position with the safety latch engaged.
  • Kingpin and apron: not bent or damaged, no space between the trailer apron and the fifth-wheel skid plate (no daylight).
  • Air and electrical lines: the service (often blue) and emergency (often red) air lines connected at the glad hands with good rubber seals, no leaks, properly secured and not tangled or rubbing; the electrical cord plugged in firmly with its locking clip and no cuts in the insulation.
  • Sliders and platform: if the fifth wheel slides, the locking pins must be fully engaged.

Trailer

On the trailer you repeat the tire/wheel/brake/suspension checks at the trailer axles, plus the landing gear (fully raised, crank handle secured, no damage), the frame and cross members, the doors and door hardware (latches, hinges, seals), mud flaps, the rear of the trailer including the DOT bumper (ICC bar), and trailer lights and reflectors.

What inspectors want to hear

The examiner is listening for the defect you'd reject the truck for. The vocabulary repeats across nearly every part, so learn the phrases rather than memorizing every component as a unique sentence:

  • Mounted parts: "securely mounted, not cracked, bent, or broken, with no missing hardware."
  • Hoses and lines: "no leaks, cracks, cuts, abrasions, or bulges, properly secured."
  • Lights and reflectors: "clean, correct color, mounted securely, and operational."
  • Tires: "properly inflated, adequate tread, no cuts, bulges, or uneven wear."
  • Fluids: "at the proper level and not leaking."

Layering one of these standard phrases onto every item gives you the "two things per item" the score sheet rewards.

Memory strategies for the long list

The list is long, but it is not random. Use structure to your advantage:

  • Group by category, not by part. Tires, rims, lug nuts, hub seals, brakes, and suspension repeat at every wheel position. Learn one "wheel station" routine and run it identically at all of them.
  • Walk a fixed loop. Always move the same direction around the truck so your body remembers the route even when your brain blanks.
  • Chunk into the seven blocks and master one block per day rather than trying to swallow everything at once.
  • Practice out loud at a real truck. Silent reading does not build the verbal recall the test demands. Touch the part as you say it; the physical association cements the memory.
  • Use the standard phrase set so you are recalling roughly 15 parts per block plus five reusable phrases, not 100 unique scripts.

Most common reasons people fail

  • Botching or forgetting the air brake check — the most frequent automatic failure.
  • Pointing without explaining — naming a part but never saying what you're checking for.
  • Missing safety-critical items: steering components, brakes, tires, and lug nuts carry heavy weight.
  • Forgetting cotter pins and castle nuts on steering linkage.
  • Reciting from memory while standing back instead of physically touching or pointing at each item.
  • Skipping an entire block, most often the engine compartment or the coupling system.
  • Vague language like "everything looks good" instead of specific defects.

What to bring and how the day works

Bring your CLP (commercial learner's permit), your regular driver's license, and any documents your state requires (medical certificate, proof of identity, the vehicle's registration and insurance, and your ELDT training certificate where required). Bring or be ready to use the supplied vehicle inspection checklist; many states allow you to hold and check off the list during the test, but you cannot pre-mark or add notes to it. Wear closed-toe shoes and clothes you don't mind getting dirty — you'll be kneeling at wheels and reaching into the engine compartment. Arrive early, make sure your test vehicle is clean and mechanically sound (a defective truck can end the test before it starts), and bring water. The examiner will brief you, then you'll work through the pre-trip, the basic control skills, and the road drive, usually in that order, over the course of roughly one to two hours.

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to memorize the whole inspection, or can I use a checklist?

Most states let you hold the official inspection checklist and check off items as you go, but you must still verbalize and demonstrate each one — you cannot just read a script silently. You're also not allowed to add your own notes to the sheet beforehand. Confirm your state's rule, because a few are stricter.

What happens if I forget one item — do I fail immediately?

Usually not. Most state versions allow a limited number of missed items before you fail the pre-trip. However, certain safety-critical areas — especially the air brake check — can be automatic failures on their own, so prioritize getting those exactly right.

How long does the pre-trip portion take?

Plan for 20 to 30 minutes for a full Class A combination inspection. Shorter for a straight truck without a trailer or coupling system.

Why do I have to say "two things" about every part?

The examiner needs evidence that you understand why you're inspecting an item, not just that you can name it. Naming the part proves recognition; describing the defect you'd reject it for proves you can judge whether the vehicle is safe. The score sheet is built around that judgment.

What's the difference between this and the written air brakes test?

The written air brakes test is multiple choice and covers how the system works. The in-cab air brake check on the pre-trip is a hands-on performance of the static leak, low-air warning, and spring-brake pop-out tests. You need the written endorsement to qualify, and you must physically perform the check during the skills test.

Can I fail the whole CDL test just on the pre-trip?

Yes. The pre-trip, basic control skills, and road test are scored separately, and you must pass all three. Failing the pre-trip ends your attempt for that day, and you'll typically need to retest (sometimes after a waiting period and a new fee).

What's the fastest way to get ready?

Practice out loud at an actual truck, walking the same fixed loop every time, and master one of the seven blocks per day. Lean on the five reusable phrases so you're recalling parts, not memorizing a hundred unique sentences. Have someone quiz you randomly so you can recover when your mind goes blank — which it will, at least once, on test day.
