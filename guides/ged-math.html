GED Math: The Complete Study Guide

The GED Math test explained — format, the calculator and formula sheet, every topic area, and a study plan for the subject people most often have to retake.

▶ Start the free GED Math practice test

For most people working toward a high school equivalency diploma, the GED Mathematical Reasoning test is the one subject that stands between them and the finish line. It is consistently the hardest of the four GED modules and the one test-takers retake more than any other. The good news is that the math itself is not advanced — there is no calculus, no trigonometry, and very little that goes beyond what you would meet in a first algebra course. What trips people up is the format, the word problems, and a few specific skills (fractions, percents, and basic algebra) that are easy to neglect. This guide walks you through exactly what the test looks like, what it covers, how it is scored, and a realistic plan for passing it on your first attempt.

How the GED Math test is structured

The Mathematical Reasoning test gives you 115 minutes (1 hour and 55 minutes) to answer 46 questions. Unlike a paper exam, the GED is delivered on a computer at an official testing center or through an approved online proctored version, so part of your preparation is simply getting comfortable with the on-screen tools.

The test is split into two parts that you take back-to-back:

  • Part 1 — no calculator: the first five questions must be solved by hand. These are designed to confirm you have solid number sense and can do basic arithmetic, estimation, and simple operations without leaning on technology.
  • Part 2 — calculator allowed: the remaining 41 questions, where an on-screen calculator is available. Once you move past the no-calculator section you cannot go back to it, so finish those five questions before you advance.

There is no separate timer for each part — the 115 minutes covers everything, and you decide how to budget your time across both sections.

Question types you will see

The GED uses several technology-enhanced question formats, not just multiple choice. Knowing them in advance keeps you from wasting time figuring out how to enter an answer:

  • Multiple choice: the most common type, with four answer options.
  • Fill-in-the-blank: you type a numeric answer directly into a box rather than picking from options — so estimating to the nearest choice will not save you.
  • Drag-and-drop: you move numbers, expressions, or steps into place, often to build an equation or order a process.
  • Hot spot: you click a location on an image, such as plotting a point on a coordinate grid or marking a value on a number line.
  • Drop-down and table-entry: you choose from an embedded menu inside a sentence or fill values into a table.

The on-screen TI-30XS calculator and formula sheet

Two built-in tools can make a real difference if you practice with them beforehand.

The TI-30XS MultiView calculator

For Part 2, an on-screen version of the Texas Instruments TI-30XS MultiView is available — it is the only calculator the GED permits, and it appears as a clickable image on your screen. You should learn its key functions before test day rather than discovering them under pressure:

  • The fraction template for entering and working with fractions directly, instead of converting everything to decimals.
  • The toggle key that switches an answer between fraction and decimal form.
  • The exponent (^) and square-root keys for powers and roots.
  • The percent function and the negative-sign key, which is separate from the subtraction key — a common source of input errors.

The makers of the GED publish a free TI-30XS reference sheet and an interactive calculator tutorial; spending an hour clicking through them is one of the highest-return things you can do.

The formula sheet

You are given an on-screen mathematics formula sheet for the entire test. It includes formulas for area and perimeter, surface area and volume of common solids, the Pythagorean theorem, slope, the quadratic formula, simple interest, and more. This means you do not need to memorize most geometry and algebra formulas. What you do need is to know which formula applies to a problem and how to plug values into it correctly. The sheet does not explain when to use each formula, so practicing with it is essential.

What the test covers

The content splits into two broad domains. Roughly 45% is quantitative problem solving and roughly 55% is algebraic problem solving. Because algebra is the larger share, it deserves the larger share of your study time — but the quantitative skills underpin everything, so you cannot skip them either.

Quantitative problem solving (about 45%)

  • Number operations and number sense: working with whole numbers, integers, and order of operations.
  • Fractions, decimals, and percents: converting between them, and solving percent-of, percent-increase, and discount problems.
  • Ratios, proportions, and rates: scaling recipes, unit rates, and comparing quantities.
  • Exponents and roots: including squares, cubes, square roots, and scientific notation.
  • Measurement and geometry: perimeter, area, volume, surface area, the Pythagorean theorem, and unit conversion.
  • Basic data and statistics: mean, median, mode, range, and reading data from tables and graphs.

Algebraic problem solving (about 55%)

  • Expressions and polynomials: evaluating, simplifying, and writing algebraic expressions; adding, multiplying, and factoring polynomials.
  • Linear equations and inequalities: solving one- and two-step equations, and equations with variables on both sides.
  • Quadratic equations: solving by factoring and by the quadratic formula on the sheet.
  • Functions: understanding function notation, evaluating functions, and comparing them.
  • Graphing and the coordinate plane: plotting points, reading graphs, and interpreting intercepts.
  • Slope and linear relationships: finding slope from points, a graph, or an equation, and writing equations of lines.

Most questions are framed as real-world word problems — budgets, distances, areas, sales, and rates — so translating English into math is itself one of the test's core skills.

Scoring and why math is the most-retaken subject

Each GED subject is scored on a scale from 100 to 200, and you need at least 145 to pass. On the math test, 145 corresponds to getting roughly 60–65% of the questions right, so you can miss a meaningful number of items and still pass. Above the passing line, two higher tiers exist: College Ready (165–174) and College Ready + Credit (175–200), which can waive placement testing or earn college credit at participating schools.

Math is the subject people retake most often, for a few reasons. It is the one area where weak foundations from years ago resurface — if fractions and basic algebra never clicked, every word problem becomes harder. The two-part calculator format and the variety of digital question types add friction that the other subjects do not have. And many adults simply spend less time on it because it feels intimidating. The encouraging flip side: because the skills are concrete and learnable, focused study moves your score more reliably here than on almost any other test.

A realistic study plan

You do not need to relearn all of high school math. You need to be solid on a focused set of skills and fluent with the test's tools. A sensible order of attack:

  • Step 1 — Lock down fractions, decimals, and percents. These appear everywhere, including the no-calculator section. If you can confidently convert among them and solve percent problems, you have removed a large chunk of test anxiety.
  • Step 2 — Build basic algebra fluency. Solving linear equations, working with expressions, and understanding slope and graphing make up the single biggest content area. Aim to solve a two-step equation almost automatically.
  • Step 3 — Learn the formula sheet and calculator. Work through practice problems with the actual formula sheet open and the TI-30XS layout in front of you, so both feel familiar rather than novel on test day.
  • Step 4 — Drill word problems. Practice translating sentences into equations. Underline what the question actually asks, identify the numbers that matter, and ignore the ones that do not.
  • Step 5 — Take full-length timed practice tests. This builds pacing (you have about 2.5 minutes per question on average) and surfaces your weak spots so you can target them.

A common timeline is six to eight weeks of steady study, roughly an hour most days, with the last week or two spent on timed full-length practice. Adjust to your starting point.

The most common mistakes

  • Ignoring the formula sheet. Some test-takers panic over formulas that are sitting right there on screen. Open the sheet early and use it.
  • Calculator input errors. Confusing the negative-sign key with subtraction, or forgetting to close a fraction template, produces wrong answers from correct math.
  • Misreading word problems. Solving for the wrong quantity — finding the price before tax when the question asks for the total — is one of the most frequent avoidable errors.
  • Skipping units and conversions. Mixing feet and inches, or minutes and hours, quietly wrecks an otherwise correct setup.
  • Poor time management. Getting stuck on one hard item early can cost you several easy points later. Flag tough questions and return to them.
  • Neglecting the no-calculator section. Because it is only five questions, people under-prepare for it — but those questions still count fully.

Calculator and formula-sheet strategy on test day

Use the tools deliberately, not reflexively. For the no-calculator five, slow down and double-check your arithmetic, since you have no machine to catch a slip. In Part 2, let the calculator handle messy computation but still estimate first — a quick mental ballpark tells you instantly when an entry error has produced an absurd answer. Keep the formula sheet open in a corner of your screen and glance at it whenever a geometry or quadratic problem appears. For fill-in-the-blank items, re-read what form the answer should take (a decimal, a whole number, a fraction) before you type. And remember you can flag a question and move on; banking the easy points first is almost always the right call.

Frequently asked questions

How many questions are on the GED Math test and how long is it?

There are 46 questions and you have 115 minutes (1 hour and 55 minutes) to finish them all.

What score do I need to pass?

You need at least 145 on the 100–200 scale, which is about 60–65% of the questions correct. Scores of 165 and above earn College Ready and College Ready + Credit recognition.

Can I use a calculator on the whole test?

No. The first five questions must be done without a calculator. For the remaining 41 questions, an on-screen TI-30XS MultiView calculator is available — it is the only model the GED allows.

Do I have to memorize formulas?

Mostly no. A formula sheet covering area, volume, slope, the Pythagorean theorem, the quadratic formula, simple interest, and more is provided on screen. You do need to know which formula to use and how to apply it.

Is the GED Math test mostly algebra or arithmetic?

Both, but algebra is slightly larger: roughly 55% algebraic problem solving and 45% quantitative problem solving. Many questions are real-world word problems that blend the two.

Why do so many people retake the math test?

Math is the most-retaken GED subject because weak foundations in fractions and algebra resurface, the two-part calculator format adds friction, and people often under-study it. Because the skills are concrete, targeted practice raises scores reliably.

How long should I study before taking it?

Most people do well with six to eight weeks of consistent study — about an hour most days — prioritizing fractions, percents, and basic algebra first, then practicing word problems and full-length timed tests near the end.
