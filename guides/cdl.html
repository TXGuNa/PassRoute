CDL Study Guide: How to Pass the Commercial Driver's License Test

Understand exactly what's on the CDL knowledge tests and how to study for them — the license classes, the required exams, endorsements, and the steps from learner's permit to license.

▶ Start the free CDL practice test

A Commercial Driver's License (CDL) is the credential that lets you legally operate large trucks, buses, and other heavy commercial vehicles in the United States. Earning one starts with the CDL knowledge test, a series of written exams administered by your state that prove you understand how to drive these vehicles safely before you ever take the wheel. This guide explains exactly what the knowledge tests cover, how the licensing process works in 2026, and how to study so you pass the first time.

What a CDL is and the three license classes

A CDL is required to drive any commercial motor vehicle that exceeds certain weight thresholds or carries passengers or hazardous materials. The federal government sets the framework through the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA), but each state's department of motor vehicles issues the license and administers the tests. There are three classes, and the one you need depends on the vehicle you intend to drive:

  • Class A: Any combination of vehicles with a gross combination weight rating (GCWR) of 26,001 pounds or more, provided the towed unit is heavier than 10,000 pounds. This is the license for tractor-trailers and most over-the-road trucking jobs, and it lets you drive Class B and C vehicles too.
  • Class B: A single vehicle with a gross vehicle weight rating (GVWR) of 26,001 pounds or more, or such a vehicle towing a unit under 10,000 pounds. Think straight trucks, large buses, dump trucks, and box trucks. A Class B holder can also drive Class C vehicles.
  • Class C: Vehicles that don't meet the Class A or B weight thresholds but are designed to carry 16 or more passengers (including the driver) or are used to transport hazardous materials requiring placards. Smaller passenger vans and certain HazMat vehicles fall here.

Who needs a CDL

You need a CDL if you operate any vehicle that meets the class definitions above. That includes long-haul truckers, delivery drivers in heavy box trucks, city and school bus drivers, dump truck and concrete mixer operators, and anyone hauling placarded hazardous materials. Age matters: you can usually get a CDL at 18 for intrastate driving (routes that never cross a state line), but federal law requires you to be at least 21 to drive interstate or to haul hazardous materials. A small federal pilot program now allows some 18-to-20-year-olds to drive interstate under supervision, but the default rule remains 21 for crossing state lines.

The three core knowledge tests

Every CDL applicant must pass written knowledge tests at a DMV or licensing office. The exact combination depends on the class and the equipment you'll drive, but three exams form the foundation.

General Knowledge

This is the universal test that every CDL applicant must pass. It is typically 50 multiple-choice questions, and you generally need 80% (40 correct) to pass. It covers a broad range of safe-driving fundamentals:

  • Pre-trip and post-trip vehicle inspection procedures
  • Defensive driving, managing space and following distance, and controlling speed for road and weather conditions
  • Safe shifting and proper backing techniques
  • Seeing hazards, communicating your intentions, and managing distracted or fatigued driving
  • Cargo loading, weight distribution, and securement
  • Hours of Service (HOS) rules, drug and alcohol regulations, and what happens during accidents or emergencies

Air Brakes

Most heavy commercial vehicles use air brakes, and you must pass this test to remove the air-brake restriction from your license. If you skip it (or fail it), you'll get an "L" restriction that legally bars you from driving any CMV equipped with full air brakes. The exam (commonly 25 questions, 80% to pass) covers:

  • Air-brake system components: compressor, governor, air tanks, brake chambers, slack adjusters, and the supply and service lines
  • Dual air-brake systems and why they exist
  • How to inspect air brakes and perform the static leakage and applied leakage checks
  • Reading the air-pressure gauge, the low-air-pressure warning, and the spring (parking) brakes
  • Brake lag, brake fade on long downgrades, and proper braking on hills

Combination Vehicles

Required for a Class A license, this test covers the unique challenges of pulling a trailer. Topics include coupling and uncoupling a tractor and trailer safely, inspecting the fifth wheel and the air and electrical lines, preventing rollovers and jackknifes, off-tracking on turns, and backing a combination unit. If you'll drive tractor-trailers, expect this test alongside General Knowledge and Air Brakes.

Endorsements and restrictions

Endorsements are add-ons that authorize you to drive specialized equipment or carry specific loads. Each requires passing an additional knowledge test, and some require more. The main endorsements are:

  • H — Hazardous Materials (HazMat): Required to haul placarded hazardous loads. This one is the most involved: you must be 21, complete HazMat entry-level driver training, pass a written test, and clear a TSA security threat assessment that includes fingerprinting and a background check. The TSA process typically takes 30 to 45 days, costs roughly $90 to $130 on top of state fees, and must be renewed every five years.
  • N — Tank Vehicles (Tanker): For hauling liquids or gases in bulk. The test emphasizes the "surge" of liquid in a partly filled tank and how it affects braking and turning.
  • T — Doubles/Triples: For pulling more than one trailer. Covers coupling multiple trailers, converter dollies, and the increased rollover risk.
  • P — Passenger: Required to drive a bus carrying passengers. Covers loading and unloading, emergency exits, and post-trip passenger checks.
  • S — School Bus: Required in addition to the P endorsement to drive a school bus. Covers loading and unloading children, the danger zone around the bus, and railroad-crossing procedures.

The combination X endorsement covers tank plus HazMat. Restrictions work in the opposite direction, limiting what you can drive. The most common is the "L" air-brake restriction (you didn't pass the air-brake test). You can also get an "E" restriction (no manual transmission) if you test in an automatic, and a "Z" restriction (no full air brakes, only air-over-hydraulic) if you test in a vehicle without a full air-brake system. Test in the type of vehicle you actually plan to drive to avoid limiting restrictions.

The CLP and ELDT: the full path to a license

You don't go straight from studying to a CDL. The process has a clear sequence.

Entry-Level Driver Training (ELDT)

Since February 7, 2022, FMCSA requires Entry-Level Driver Training for anyone obtaining a Class A or Class B CDL for the first time, upgrading from Class B to Class A, or getting a HazMat (H), Passenger (P), or School Bus (S) endorsement for the first time. ELDT has two parts: theory (classroom or online instruction) and behind-the-wheel (practical) training. Crucially, you must train with a provider listed on FMCSA's Training Provider Registry (TPR) — training from an unregistered source does not count. For most CDL classes, the theory portion must be completed before you take the knowledge test, so plan your training early.

Commercial Learner's Permit (CLP)

After passing the required knowledge tests (and meeting medical and identity requirements), you receive a Commercial Learner's Permit. The CLP lets you practice driving a commercial vehicle on public roads, but only with a CDL holder seated beside you. Federal rules require you to hold the CLP for a minimum of 14 days before you can take the skills (road) test. The CLP is generally valid for up to a year. Once you pass the three-part skills test — a vehicle inspection, a basic control test, and an on-road driving test — your CLP converts to a full CDL.

Test format, scoring, and how passing works

The knowledge tests are multiple-choice and most states now deliver them on a computer at the DMV. The General Knowledge test is usually 50 questions; the endorsement and air-brake tests are commonly around 20 to 30 questions each. The standard passing score is 80% across the board, so on a 50-question exam you need 40 correct, and on a 25-question exam you need 20 correct. A few states vary slightly, so confirm with your local DMV. If you fail a section, you typically retake only that section, often after a short waiting period and a small re-test fee. You generally must pass all the written tests for your license class before the DMV will issue your CLP.

A realistic study plan

Most motivated test-takers can be ready in two to four weeks studying an hour a day. Here's an effective approach:

  • Week 1 — Read the handbook. Download your state's official CDL manual (free from your DMV). It is the single source the test is written from. Read the General Knowledge, Air Brakes, and Combination Vehicles sections relevant to your class.
  • Week 2 — Practice tests. Take free practice tests repeatedly until you consistently score above 90%. Practice tests reveal which topics you've actually retained versus what you only think you know.
  • Week 3 — Drill weak spots and memorize the inspection. Focus on the sections you keep missing, and start memorizing the pre-trip inspection routine word for word, since the skills test demands it later.
  • Before test day — Review air-brake leakage rates, low-air warning thresholds, and stopping-distance concepts, which are the most commonly missed details.

The most common mistakes test-takers make

  • Studying generic material instead of your state's handbook. Rules and even some numbers vary by state. Always study from your own DMV's current manual.
  • Skipping the Air Brakes test. Many new drivers don't realize that failing or skipping it triggers the L restriction, which makes them unhireable for most trucking jobs.
  • Cramming the pre-trip inspection. The written test touches on inspection, but the skills test requires you to recite it. Start early.
  • Confusing the air-brake leakage rates. The allowable pressure-drop limits differ for single versus combination vehicles and for static versus applied tests. These trip up a large share of test-takers.
  • Misjudging stopping distance. Forgetting that total stopping distance combines perception distance, reaction distance, and braking distance — and that it grows dramatically with speed and weight — costs easy points.
  • Ignoring ELDT timing. Some applicants book the knowledge test before completing required theory training and get turned away.

Air-brake and pre-trip specifics worth knowing

A few facts appear on nearly every air-brake and pre-trip section, so commit them to memory:

  • The low-air-pressure warning must activate before system pressure drops below 60 psi.
  • The spring (emergency/parking) brakes automatically engage when air pressure falls to roughly 20 to 45 psi — never rely on them as a service brake.
  • The air compressor governor typically cuts out around 120 to 140 psi and cuts back in around 85 to 100 psi.
  • For the static air-leakage test, with the engine off and brakes released, the loss should be no more than about 2 psi per minute for a single vehicle and 3 psi per minute for a combination vehicle (slightly higher with the brakes applied).
  • Always check slack adjuster travel and confirm brake-chamber pushrod movement is within limits.
  • On long downgrades, use a lower gear and steady, light braking to avoid brake fade from overheating — never ride the brakes.

For the pre-trip inspection, learn a consistent walk-around order (engine compartment, in-cab, lights, then the full exterior front-to-back) so you never skip a component. Always describe what you're checking and what would make it unsafe.

Day-of-test tips

  • Bring all required documents: proof of identity, Social Security verification, proof of residency, and your valid DOT medical examiner's certificate.
  • Arrive early and well-rested. The tests are not timed in most states, so read each question carefully and don't rush.
  • Eliminate obviously wrong answers first; CDL questions often have one clearly correct choice once you remove the distractors.
  • Watch for "all of the above" and "none of the above" answers, which are frequently correct on safety-rule questions.
  • If you fail one section, don't panic — most states let you retake just that section, sometimes the same day or after a short wait.

Frequently asked questions

How many questions are on the CDL general knowledge test?

The General Knowledge test is typically 50 multiple-choice questions, and you usually need 80% — 40 correct answers — to pass. Endorsement and air-brake tests are shorter, often 20 to 30 questions, also requiring 80%.

What score do I need to pass the CDL knowledge test?

The standard passing score is 80% on each written test. A few states use a slightly different threshold, so confirm with your local DMV before you test.

How long does it take to get a CDL?

It varies, but expect a few weeks to a couple of months. You must complete ELDT theory training, pass the knowledge tests to get your CLP, hold the CLP for at least 14 days, and then pass the skills test. Full-time CDL schools often compress behind-the-wheel training into three to eight weeks.

Do I have to take the air brakes test?

You don't have to, but if you don't pass it you'll receive an "L" restriction that legally prevents you from driving any commercial vehicle with full air brakes. Since most trucks and buses use air brakes, nearly every applicant should take and pass it.

What is ELDT and do I need it?

Entry-Level Driver Training is FMCSA-mandated training required since February 7, 2022 for first-time Class A or B applicants, Class B-to-A upgrades, and first-time HazMat, Passenger, or School Bus endorsements. You must train with a provider on FMCSA's Training Provider Registry, and theory training generally must be done before your knowledge test.

How old do I have to be to get a CDL?

You can typically get a CDL at 18 for intrastate driving within your state. Federal law requires you to be 21 to drive across state lines (interstate) or to haul hazardous materials, though a federal pilot program allows some supervised 18-to-20-year-olds to drive interstate.

What's the difference between a CLP and a CDL?

A Commercial Learner's Permit (CLP) lets you practice driving a commercial vehicle on public roads only with a licensed CDL holder beside you. You earn it by passing the written tests. After holding it at least 14 days and passing the skills test, it becomes a full CDL that lets you drive on your own.

How many times can I retake the CDL test if I fail?

Most states allow multiple retakes, usually after a short waiting period and a small re-test fee, and you typically retake only the section you failed rather than the entire exam. Check your state's specific limits and fees.
