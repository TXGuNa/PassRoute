Sample questions

I currently hold a Class A CDL and routinely haul a single loaded semi-trailer. My company now wants me to pull a set of two trailers connected by a converter dolly on interstate runs. Before I can legally drive this combination in Texas, which of the following must I obtain?

  1. A doubles/triples (T) endorsement, because pulling more than one trailer requires it ✓ Correct answer
  2. A tank (N) endorsement, since the converter dolly adds an air tank to the combination
  3. Nothing extra, because my Class A CDL already covers any number of trailers
  4. A hazardous materials (H) endorsement, because multi-trailer sets are treated as oversize loads

Why: Pulling more than one trailer requires a doubles/triples (T) endorsement, no matter what your license class is.

I just upgraded from a single 53-foot van to pulling a set of doubles on the same Texas interstate route I've driven for years. As I think about how this rig will behave compared to my old single trailer, which statement should shape how I drive?

  1. The two trailers track tighter through curves, so I can hold my old single-trailer speeds and lane-change timing
  2. The set is harder to control and more prone to rollover, so I must steer gently and slow down before turns ✓ Correct answer
  3. Because the load is split between two boxes, the rig is lighter overall and noticeably slower to react
  4. With a second trailer adding length, crosswinds affect the set far less than they did my single van

Why: Doubles and triples are less stable and more prone to rollover, so steer gently and slow down before turns.

I'm hooking a loaded second trailer behind my lead trailer at the yard. Before I can pull the set, I have to position the piece of equipment that carries its own drawbar, fifth wheel, locking jaws, air lines, and safety chains so the rear trailer can ride on it. Which component am I coupling to join the second trailer to the first?

  1. The kingpin, which locks down into the fifth wheel of the trailer ahead of it
  2. The glad hands, which join the two trailers and let air reach the rear brakes
  3. The converter dolly, which carries the fifth wheel and couples the next trailer ✓ Correct answer
  4. The pintle hook, which the dolly's drawbar latches into to hold the trailers together

Why: The converter dolly carries the fifth wheel and couples one trailer to the next, with its own drawbar, locking jaws, air lines, and safety chains.

I'm pulling a set of doubles on the interstate at highway speed when a car suddenly cuts into my lane, and I jerk the wheel hard to avoid it. The tractor and front trailer stay upright and tracking fine, but I need to anticipate what's happening behind me. Which of the following is the MOST likely result of that quick steering input?

  1. The rear trailer's brakes lock up and drag it straight, helping the whole set slow down and stabilize
  2. The extra trailers add weight low to the ground, so the set actually resists rolling better than a single
  3. The rear trailer is pushed down onto its suspension and settles, keeping it planted through the maneuver
  4. The rear trailer swings out much wider than the tractor and can crack the whip and roll over ✓ Correct answer

Why: A quick steering move causes rearward amplification, making the rear trailer swing much wider than the tractor and possibly roll over.

You are pulling a set of doubles on a rural Texas highway at 55 mph when a vehicle suddenly drifts into your lane ahead. To minimize the risk of rearward amplification (the "crack-the-whip" effect) rolling over your rear trailer, what should you do?

  1. Steer gently and gradually while easing off the accelerator to keep the rear trailer tracking smoothly ✓ Correct answer
  2. Make one quick, firm steering correction to clear the hazard before the rear trailer reacts
  3. Brake hard and hold a sharp steering input to stop short of the drifting vehicle
  4. Maintain your speed and steer aggressively so the trailers stay aligned behind the tractor

Why: Because rearward amplification grows with each trailer, steer gently and gradually and ease off the speed to keep the rear trailer tracking smoothly.

I've just finished coupling a set of doubles and I'm walking the rig before pulling out. I open the shut-off (cut-out) valves between the tractor and the front trailer, and between the two trailers, so air can reach the back of the set. When I reach the very rear of the LAST trailer, what position must that shut-off valve be in for the set to be configured correctly?

  1. Open, so the service air can vent freely and keep the rear brakes from dragging while I drive
  2. Closed, because it is the last valve in the line and leaving it open would dump the air and prevent the system from building pressure ✓ Correct answer
  3. Open, the same as every other shut-off valve in the set, so air pressure stays equal across all trailers
  4. Closed only on triples, but left open on a two-trailer set since the air just stops at the rear axle

Why: The shut-off valve at the rear of the very last trailer must be closed, or air would escape out the back and the system could not build brake pressure.
