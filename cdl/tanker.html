Sample questions

I drive several different loads each week, and I'm trying to figure out which job requires me to add a tank vehicle (N) endorsement to my CDL. For which of the following hauls would the N endorsement be required?

  1. Transporting liquids or gases inside a permanently mounted bulk tank on the vehicle ✓ Correct answer
  2. Pulling a dry van loaded with boxed, palletized freight that shifts in transit
  3. Hauling a refrigerated trailer of fresh produce that must stay cold
  4. Driving an empty tank trailer back to the yard after it has been fully drained

Why: The N (tank vehicle) endorsement is required to haul liquids or gases in a permanently mounted bulk tank because of the high center of gravity and surge hazards.

I'm hauling a partly loaded liquid tanker and I stop at a red light at the bottom of a downhill grade. When the light turns green and I begin to pull away, I need to be especially prepared for the one factor that makes a liquid tank harder to control than a comparable dry-bulk load. Which factor is that?

  1. The tank's bright reflective paint makes it harder to judge clearances at night
  2. The liquid surging back and forth inside the tank, which can shove the truck forward ✓ Correct answer
  3. The faster tire wear caused by carrying liquid instead of dry freight
  4. The unusually light overall weight of a liquid load compared to dry bulk

Why: In a partly filled tank the liquid surges back and forth, and the forward surge after a stop can shove the truck ahead.

I'm loading a tank trailer with a cold liquid at a terminal on a hot Texas afternoon, and the loader tells me he is leaving the top of the tank partly empty rather than filling it to the very top. He says this gap has a specific name and a specific reason. What is this intentional empty space called, and why is it left?

  1. Surge space, left so the liquid has room to slosh back and forth and reduce the forward push when I brake
  2. Ballast space, left empty so I can add weight later to keep the high center of gravity stable in curves
  3. Outage, left so the liquid has room to expand as it warms during the trip ✓ Correct answer
  4. Overflow, left empty so the tank can vent excess vapor through the manhole without leaking

Why: The empty space left at the top of a liquid tank is called outage, and it gives the liquid room to expand as it warms.

I'm hauling a full load of liquid in a tank trailer and approaching a posted highway off-ramp marked 35 mph. Compared with the dry-box trailers I usually pull, why must I take this ramp well below the posted speed?

  1. Because the empty space (outage) left above the liquid shifts the load rearward, lightening the steer axle
  2. Because liquid weighs less than dry freight, so my brakes have far less traction to slow the rig
  3. Because a full tank eliminates surge, leaving the suspension with nothing to absorb the cornering force
  4. Because the liquid rides high in the tank, giving the rig a high center of gravity that rolls over easily ✓ Correct answer

Why: Liquid cargo rides high in the tank, giving the rig a high center of gravity that makes it roll over easily.

You are hauling a partially loaded tank of liquid down a highway and see a posted off-ramp ahead marked '35 MPH.' Because your tank vehicle has a high center of gravity, what is the safest way to handle the ramp?

  1. Reduce your speed to well below the posted 35 MPH before you enter the ramp ✓ Correct answer
  2. Maintain the posted 35 MPH through the ramp since that limit is set for safe travel
  3. Wait until you are in the curve, then brake firmly to scrub off speed
  4. Keep your speed up entering the ramp and steer sharply to stay in your lane

Why: Because tank vehicles have a high center of gravity, slow well below the posted speed and brake gently before entering a ramp or curve to prevent rollover.

I'm hauling a load in a tank trailer, and during my pre-trip I notice the inside is divided into several fully separate sections by solid walls running across the tank, with no openings between the sections. When the examiner asks me to identify this internal design feature, which term correctly describes those solid dividing walls?

  1. Baffles, since they are the internal walls placed inside a tank to control how the liquid moves around
  2. Bulkheads, since they are solid walls that split the tank into completely separate compartments ✓ Correct answer
  3. A smooth bore design, since the tank's interior has no internal hardware interrupting the liquid
  4. Vents, since they are the internal fittings that separate the tank into sealed pressure sections

Why: Bulkheads are solid walls that divide a tank into completely separate compartments with no openings between them.
