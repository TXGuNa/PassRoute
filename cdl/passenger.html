Sample questions

I just passed my CDL skills test and I'm picking up my first paid run tomorrow. My boss hands me the keys to a vehicle and asks which one I'm legally allowed to operate with ONLY my passenger (P) endorsement on top of my CDL. Which of these vehicles actually requires that P endorsement?

  1. A motorcoach designed to seat the passenger threshold, even with few riders today ✓ Correct answer
  2. A loaded box truck hauling general freight on an interstate route with no passengers aboard
  3. A flatbed combination rig pulling a single trailer of secured construction materials
  4. A heavy crew-cab pickup towing an enclosed equipment trailer to a job site with three coworkers riding along

Why: The passenger (P) endorsement is required based on a vehicle's designed seating capacity, not how many seats are actually filled on a given trip.

During the pre-trip, passenger items you must check include:

  1. The favourite local radio station
  2. Handrails, seats, exits, and signaling devices ✓ Correct answer
  3. The exterior body paint color
  4. The plastic drink cup holders

Why: Check passenger safety equipment before driving.

My city transit bus is full during rush hour and several passengers have to stand in the aisle. To keep my standees positioned legally and avoid blocking my view, where must I make sure none of these standing riders are located?

  1. Behind the white standee line near the rear doors
  2. Forward of the standee line toward the front of the bus ✓ Correct answer
  3. Within reach of a grab rail or stanchion in the aisle
  4. Beside the rear emergency exit window row

Why: Riders must stand behind the standee line, so no passenger may stand forward of it toward the front of the bus where the driver's view is blocked.

A passenger boarding my charter bus asks me to stow a small drum of hazardous material in the cargo bay for the trip. Before I agree, which understanding of the rules should guide my decision?

  1. Hazardous materials may ride on any bus as long as I keep them sealed and stored under the passenger seats where people can watch them.
  2. Any hazardous material is fine to carry as long as the total load stays at or below 500 lb in the cargo bay.
  3. Most hazardous materials are forbidden on a bus, and only a few specified materials in small, limited amounts may ever be carried. ✓ Correct answer
  4. Hazardous materials are required to be transported by bus whenever a passenger requests it, provided each one is under 100 lb.

Why: A bus may not carry most hazardous materials, and only a few specified materials in small, limited amounts are ever permitted aboard.

During my pre-trip walkthrough of a loaded charter bus, a passenger hands me a large duffel bag and asks me to find a spot for it before we depart. Under Texas DPS rules for securing carry-on baggage, which condition is the MOST important reason I must reject placing it where the passenger first suggested?

  1. The bag would press against the upholstery of two nearby seats
  2. The bag would feel cold sitting near the floor heating vents
  3. The bag would look untidy resting on top of the seat cushion
  4. The bag would partially block the center aisle and a side exit ✓ Correct answer

Why: Carry-on baggage must be secured so it never blocks the aisle or any exit, keeping passengers able to move and evacuate freely in an emergency.

I'm driving a loaded transit bus and approaching an active railroad crossing with no signal, gate, or flagger. To handle it correctly, what should I do as I reach the tracks?

  1. Stop between 15 and 50 feet before the nearest rail, look and listen, then cross without shifting gears ✓ Correct answer
  2. Slow down, downshift into a lower gear, and roll across steadily so I keep momentum over the rails
  3. Stop right at the rail closest to me, look and listen, then accelerate hard to clear both tracks
  4. Only stop if a train is in sight or I hear a horn; otherwise treat it like a normal intersection and proceed

Why: At an unmarked railroad crossing a bus must stop between 15 and 50 feet before the nearest rail, look and listen, and never shift gears while crossing.
