Question 1

During your pre-trip inspection, you measure tread depth and find one of your steering-axle (front) tires has worn down to 3/32 inch, while your drive and trailer tires are all at 3/32 inch as well. Which statement correctly identifies whether your vehicle is legal to drive?

  1. The vehicle is NOT legal to drive, because steering-axle tires must have at least 4/32 inch of tread. ✓
  2. The vehicle is legal to drive, because all tires meet the 2/32 inch minimum for every tire.
  3. The vehicle is legal to drive, because the front tires only need 2/32 inch and the rest are fine.
  4. The vehicle is NOT legal to drive, because all tires on every axle must have at least 4/32 inch of tread.
Correct answer: A. The vehicle is NOT legal to drive, because steering-axle tires must have at least 4/32 inch of tread.
Why: Steering-axle tires must have at least 4/32 inch of tread, so a front tire at 3/32 inch is below the minimum and makes the vehicle illegal to drive.
Question 2

During your pre-trip inspection you run a tread-depth gauge across all your tires. The steering-axle tires measure a healthy 5/32 inch, but one of your rear drive tires is worn down to a noticeably thinner reading. What is the LEAST tread depth that rear (non-steering) tire can legally have and still pass inspection?

  1. 1/32 inch
  2. 2/32 inch ✓
  3. 4/32 inch
  4. 6/32 inch
Correct answer: B. 2/32 inch
Why: All tires except the front steering axle must have at least 2/32 inch of tread depth measured in a major groove.
Question 3

During your pre-trip inspection you grip the steering wheel and rock it back and forth before starting the engine. On your 20-inch diameter wheel, you can turn the rim a couple of inches in each direction before the front wheels begin to respond. At what point should this free play make you suspect a steering-system problem and pull the vehicle from service?

  1. When the wheel can be turned more than about 10 degrees (roughly 2 inches on a 20-inch wheel) before the front wheels move ✓
  2. Only when the wheel can be turned more than about 20 degrees before the front wheels move
  3. When the wheel can be turned more than about 5 degrees, since any detectable play is unsafe
  4. Only when free play exceeds about 45 degrees, since that is when steering becomes truly hard to control
Correct answer: A. When the wheel can be turned more than about 10 degrees (roughly 2 inches on a 20-inch wheel) before the front wheels move
Why: More than about 10 degrees of steering-wheel free play (roughly 2 inches on a 20-inch wheel) before the front wheels move is too much and signals a steering problem.
Question 4

Your tractor-trailer has stalled on the shoulder of a two-lane undivided highway and won't restart. You switch on your hazard flashers and reach for your emergency warning kit so you can set out reflective triangles at 100 ft behind, 100 ft ahead, and 10 ft to the rear of the vehicle. To be legally equipped to place warning devices in EVERY required position like this, what is the MINIMUM number of reflective triangles your truck must carry?

  1. Two triangles, since one is placed ahead and one behind the vehicle
  2. Three triangles ✓
  3. One triangle placed 10 ft to the rear, plus your hazard flashers
  4. Six triangles, to be sure you can cover both directions of traffic
Correct answer: B. Three triangles
Why: A commercial vehicle must carry at least three reflective warning triangles as emergency warning devices.
Question 5

During my pre-trip inspection, an examiner asks me to explain the air brake setup on my tractor. I tell him the air brakes are really several braking systems combined into one. Which answer correctly states how many separate braking systems an air brake system actually combines, and names them?

  1. Three systems: the service brakes, the parking brakes, and the emergency brakes. ✓
  2. Two systems: the primary air system and the secondary air system with one set of controls.
  3. Four systems: the service brakes, the parking brakes, the emergency brakes, and the ABS.
  4. One system: a single circuit that handles normal stops, parking, and emergencies together.
Correct answer: A. Three systems: the service brakes, the parking brakes, and the emergency brakes.
Why: An air brake system is actually three separate systems combined: the service brakes, the parking brakes, and the emergency brakes.
Question 6

I'm doing my pre-trip inspection and I rev the engine to build air pressure while watching the dash gauge. I want to confirm the governor is working correctly by noting the pressure at which the air compressor stops pumping air into the tanks. At approximately what reading should the needle stop climbing because the governor has cut OUT?

  1. About 100 psi, which is the pressure where the compressor stops adding air to the tanks
  2. About 125 psi, at which point the governor cuts out and the compressor stops pumping ✓
  3. About 150 psi, where the system reaches its maximum and the compressor shuts off
  4. About 60 psi, the point at which the governor halts the compressor on a charged system
Correct answer: B. About 125 psi, at which point the governor cuts out and the compressor stops pumping
Why: The governor cuts out at about 125 psi, stopping the compressor from pumping more air into the tanks.
Question 7

I'm parked doing my pre-trip and watching the air gauge. The compressor had cut out earlier and stopped pumping, so the pressure has been slowly dropping as I test the system. At what gauge reading should I expect the governor to kick the compressor back in and start pumping air again?

  1. About 60 psi, the same point where the low air pressure warning light and buzzer come on
  2. About 125 psi, the level at which the governor stops the compressor from pumping
  3. About 100 psi, the point where the governor signals the compressor to resume pumping ✓
  4. About 150 psi, the level at which the safety relief valve opens to release pressure
Correct answer: C. About 100 psi, the point where the governor signals the compressor to resume pumping
Why: The governor cut-in point is about 100 psi, where it signals the compressor to resume pumping air.
Question 8

You are driving a 53-foot tractor-trailer and approaching a tight right turn at a city intersection. As you begin to steer the tractor through the turn, where will your trailer's rear wheels track relative to the path your front (tractor) wheels are following?

  1. The rear wheels will track a tighter, inner path than the front wheels, so I must swing wide to let the trailer clear the curb. ✓
  2. The rear wheels will track a wider, outer path than the front wheels, so I should hug the inside of the turn.
  3. The rear wheels will follow the exact same path as the front wheels, so I can turn as if driving a straight truck.
  4. The rear wheels will not follow any predictable path, so I should brake hard mid-turn to stay in control.
Correct answer: A. The rear wheels will track a tighter, inner path than the front wheels, so I must swing wide to let the trailer clear the curb.
Why: The trailer's rear wheels track a tighter inner path than the front wheels (off-tracking), so swing wide enough for the trailer to clear the corner.
Question 9

I'm driving a tractor-trailer and need to make a tight right turn at a city intersection. I know my trailer's rear wheels off-track and follow a tighter path than my steer axle, and I want to keep the trailer from climbing the curb. What is the BEST way to steer through this turn?

  1. Swing the tractor wide to the left first to open up room, then complete the turn
  2. Keep the rear of the trailer close to the curb and swing wide only as you finish the turn ✓
  3. Begin turning the wheel sharply and early so the whole rig pivots before reaching the corner
  4. Drift toward the center line ahead of the corner so the trailer has space to cut inside
Correct answer: B. Keep the rear of the trailer close to the curb and swing wide only as you finish the turn
Why: Because of off-tracking, keep the trailer's rear near the curb and swing wide only as you complete the turn so it clears the corner.
Question 10

I'm doing a pre-trip inspection on my tractor-trailer and I trace the air hoses running from the back of the cab to the front of the trailer. As I follow them to the glad-hand connectors, how many air lines should I expect to find joining the two units?

  1. Three lines: a service line, an emergency line, and a separate ABS line
  2. One line, since a single supply hose feeds both the service and emergency systems
  3. Two lines: the service (control) line and the emergency (supply) line ✓
  4. Four lines, one for each pair of glad-hand connectors on the units
Correct answer: C. Two lines: the service (control) line and the emergency (supply) line
Why: A tractor and trailer are joined by exactly two air lines: the service (control) line and the emergency (supply) line.
Question 11

  1. To contain the material so it cannot escape, and to communicate the danger so others can stay clear and respond safely ✓
  2. To guarantee faster delivery windows by giving placarded loads priority access to highway lanes and routes
  3. To document each shipment so the carrier can bill the customer the correct hazardous-handling surcharge
  4. To reduce the carrier's fuel costs by routing heavy hazardous loads onto the shortest available roads
Correct answer: A. To contain the material so it cannot escape, and to communicate the danger so others can stay clear and respond safely

Question 12

I just got hired to run a route hauling a placarded load of corrosive material, and I already hold a Class A CDL with a tanker endorsement. Before I can legally take this trip, which of the following must I do?

  1. Nothing extra — my Class A CDL with the tanker endorsement already covers any placarded load I haul
  2. Obtain the hazardous materials (H) endorsement, which requires passing a TSA security threat assessment and fingerprinting ✓
  3. Add a doubles/triples endorsement, since placarded loads must be split across multiple trailers
  4. Add a passenger endorsement, because placarded loads are regulated the same way passenger transport is
Correct answer: B. Obtain the hazardous materials (H) endorsement, which requires passing a TSA security threat assessment and fingerprinting
Why: Hauling any placardable quantity of hazardous materials requires the hazardous materials (H) endorsement, which requires a TSA security threat assessment and fingerprinting.
