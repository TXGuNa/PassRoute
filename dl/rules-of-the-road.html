Sample questions

You are driving on a rural Texas highway posted at 70 mph. A sudden, heavy fog rolls in and you can barely see two car lengths ahead. Under Texas law, what speed are you permitted to drive?

  1. Slower than 70 mph — a speed that is reasonable and safe for the fog, because the posted limit is the maximum for ideal conditions only ✓ Correct answer
  2. Up to 70 mph, because that is the legally posted limit and you may always drive the posted speed
  3. Exactly 50 mph, because Texas law requires reducing speed by 20 mph whenever visibility is poor
  4. Up to 75 mph, since rural Texas interstates allow 75 mph regardless of conditions

Why: Under Texas's prima facie (basic speed law), you must never drive faster than is reasonable and safe for existing conditions, so in heavy fog you must slow well below the posted 70 mph. Driving the full posted limit (B) is the trap: the posted number is a maximum for good conditions, not a guarantee, and the 20-mph and 75-mph figures (C, D) are real rules pulled into the wrong context.

You turn off a posted highway onto a residential street inside a city's urban district. You see no speed limit sign anywhere along the block. What speed should you assume is the legal maximum here?

  1. 20 mph, because that is the default in any area where homes are present
  2. 30 mph, the common default limit for an urban district when no sign is posted ✓ Correct answer
  3. 70 mph, since that is the default for highways and no other sign overrides it
  4. There is no limit as long as you drive safely, because the sign is missing

Why: Where no sign is posted, the common default speed limit in an urban district is 30 mph, so you should treat 30 mph as the legal maximum. The 20 mph figure is the typical reduced limit for active school zones, not residential streets generally, and 70 mph is the daytime default for numbered highways, not a city street.

You are driving on a numbered Texas highway in the middle of the afternoon. Conditions are clear and dry, but you have not seen a single posted speed limit sign for several miles. What is the maximum speed you may legally drive under the basic speed law?

  1. 75 mph, because that is the limit on all rural Texas highways
  2. 30 mph, because that is the default limit where signs are absent
  3. 70 mph, the default daytime limit on numbered highways ✓ Correct answer
  4. 85 mph, the highest speed allowed anywhere in Texas

Why: Where no sign is posted, the default daytime limit on numbered highways in Texas is 70 mph. The 30 mph trap is the default for urban districts, not numbered highways, and 75 and 85 mph apply only to specific posted Interstates and tollways, not as a general default.

You are driving on a rural Texas Interstate posted at 75 mph. After a few miles you transition onto a specific Texas tollway, and the posted signs now read 85 mph. Traffic is light, the pavement is dry, and visibility is clear. What is the maximum speed you may legally drive on this tollway stretch?

  1. 70 mph, because that is the statewide daytime maximum for numbered highways
  2. 75 mph, because that was the limit on the Interstate you just left
  3. 80 mph, because Texas law caps all roadways at 80 mph
  4. 85 mph, because that is the speed posted on this specific tollway ✓ Correct answer

Why: On Texas roads you may legally drive up to the posted speed limit when conditions are safe, and some specific Texas tollways are posted as high as 80 to 85 mph. The 70 and 75 mph options are real limits for other roads but do not override the higher posted sign on this tollway.

You're driving past an elementary school at 7:45 a.m. The posted school-zone sign reads '20 MPH WHEN FLASHING,' and the yellow beacons on it are blinking. What is the correct speed to drive here?

  1. 20 mph, because you must obey the posted reduced school-zone limit while the lights are flashing ✓ Correct answer
  2. 30 mph, the default urban speed limit, since the school zone speed only applies to the curb lane
  3. Any safe speed under the regular limit, because flashing beacons are only a warning, not a law
  4. 70 mph, the daytime highway limit, since the reduced limit applies only after children exit the building

Why: When school-zone lights are flashing (or children are present), you must obey the posted reduced limit, typically 20 mph. The 30 mph default applies only on ordinary urban streets without a posted school-zone sign, so it is the wrong limit here.

You are driving past an elementary school in the morning. The school-zone lights are flashing and children are on the sidewalk. Your phone rings with a call from work. What does Texas law require you to do?

  1. Answer the call, since the school-zone speed limit only restricts how fast you may drive
  2. Leave the handheld phone alone, because using a handheld phone is illegal in an active school zone ✓ Correct answer
  3. Answer the call but slow to 20 mph below the posted limit while talking
  4. Pull over within 15 feet of the school crosswalk to take the call safely

Why: In an active school zone (lights flashing or children present), Texas law prohibits using a handheld phone, so you must not answer the call. Option A is wrong because the rule covers phone use, not just speed; the 20-mph-below figure in C is the Move Over/Slow Down rule for stopped emergency vehicles, not school zones.
