Sample questions

You're following a sedan on a dry highway. As its rear bumper passes a roadside sign, you start counting; you reach the same sign just under 2 seconds later. What does this tell you, and what should you do?

  1. You are following too closely; ease off the gas to open the gap to at least 3 seconds. ✓ Correct answer
  2. Your distance is safe because 2 seconds is the recommended minimum on dry roads.
  3. Your distance is fine; the 3-second rule only applies in rain, fog, or ice.
  4. You should brake hard immediately to drop back, since you are within the truck's No-Zone.

Why: The three-second rule says that if you reach the fixed point in less than 3 seconds after the car ahead, you are too close, so easing off the gas to restore a 3-second gap is correct. Two seconds is not the safe minimum, the 3-second rule applies in all conditions (you add more seconds for bad weather), and braking hard or worrying about a truck's No-Zone does not fit this car-following scenario.

You normally leave a 3-second gap behind the car ahead. Tonight it starts raining hard on the freeway and a thick fog rolls in. What should you do about your following distance?

  1. Keep the same 3-second gap, since the three-second rule already covers all driving conditions
  2. Increase the gap to more than 3 seconds, because rain and fog reduce traction and visibility ✓ Correct answer
  3. Reduce the gap so you can keep the car ahead's taillights in clear view through the fog
  4. Switch on your high beams and hold a 2-second gap to react faster to its brake lights

Why: The three-second rule is a minimum for good conditions; in rain, fog, ice, or at high speed you must add more seconds because traction and visibility are reduced. Shortening the gap or relying on high beams (which reflect back off fog) only increases your danger.

You are driving on a Texas highway when a pickup truck moves in close behind you and stays just a few feet off your bumper, clearly tailgating. What is the safest way to handle the space around your vehicle?

  1. Tap your brakes a few times to warn the tailgater to back off
  2. Speed up so the tailgater falls farther behind you
  3. Increase the following distance between you and the vehicle ahead so you have more room to brake gradually ✓ Correct answer
  4. Stay exactly three seconds behind the car ahead, since that rule already covers every situation

Why: When you are being tailgated, you should increase your following distance to the vehicle ahead so you can slow down or stop gradually instead of braking hard, giving the tailgater more time to react. Three seconds is only a minimum baseline and should be increased behind motorcycles, large trucks, and when tailgated, so option D is wrong, and brake-tapping or speeding up only raises the risk of a crash.

You are driving on a multi-lane highway alongside an 18-wheeler, staying just behind its cab where you cannot see the truck driver's side mirrors. What does this position tell you, and what should you do?

  1. The truck driver can see you fine, so you may stay there as long as you keep a steady speed
  2. You should move closer to the trailer, since the safest spot is directly beside the rear wheels
  3. You should flash your high beams so the truck driver knows you are there before passing
  4. You are in the truck's blind spot (a No-Zone) because you can't see the driver's mirrors, so speed up or drop back to get out of it ✓ Correct answer

Why: If you can't see a large truck's side mirrors, the driver can't see you, meaning you are sitting in a No-Zone blind spot; the safe response is to clear it quickly by speeding up to pass or easing back. Staying put, moving beside the rear wheels, or relying on high-beam flashes all leave you in the blind spot where the driver cannot see you.

Driving on a Texas highway, you spot a stalled car ahead and bring your vehicle to a complete stop. Thinking back, you realize the whole stop took longer than you expected. Which three distances added together make up the total distance your car traveled from the moment the hazard appeared until you were fully stopped?

  1. Perception distance + reaction distance + braking distance ✓ Correct answer
  2. Perception distance + reaction distance + following distance
  3. Reaction distance + braking distance + hydroplaning distance
  4. Following distance + braking distance + headlight sight distance

Why: Total stopping distance is the sum of perception distance (seeing the hazard), reaction distance (moving your foot to the brake), and braking distance (the car slowing to a stop). Following distance, hydroplaning, and headlight sight distance are real safe-driving concepts but are not components of the stopping-distance formula.

A deer suddenly steps into the road ahead of you on a dry highway. From the moment your eyes register the deer to the instant your foot actually starts pressing the brake, your car keeps rolling forward. About how long does that reaction time typically take for the average driver?

  1. About 1.5 seconds
  2. About three-quarters (¾) of a second ✓ Correct answer
  3. About 3 seconds
  4. About 35 seconds

Why: The average driver's reaction time is about ¾ of a second, so the car keeps moving during that interval before braking begins. Option A (about 1.5 s) is the combined perception-plus-reaction time, and 3 seconds is the safe-following-distance rule, not reaction time.
