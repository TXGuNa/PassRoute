Sample questions

I just earned my H endorsement and a new driver asks me why all the placarding, shipping-paper, and packaging rules even exist. Of the following, which BEST describes the main purpose behind the hazardous materials regulations?

  1. To contain the material so it cannot escape, and to communicate the danger so others can stay clear and respond safely ✓ Correct answer
  2. To guarantee faster delivery windows by giving placarded loads priority access to highway lanes and routes
  3. To document each shipment so the carrier can bill the customer the correct hazardous-handling surcharge
  4. To reduce the carrier's fuel costs by routing heavy hazardous loads onto the shortest available roads

Why: HazMat rules exist to contain the material so it cannot escape and to communicate the danger so others can stay clear and respond safely.

I just got hired to run a route hauling a placarded load of corrosive material, and I already hold a Class A CDL with a tanker endorsement. Before I can legally take this trip, which of the following must I do?

  1. Nothing extra — my Class A CDL with the tanker endorsement already covers any placarded load I haul
  2. Obtain the hazardous materials (H) endorsement, which requires passing a TSA security threat assessment and fingerprinting ✓ Correct answer
  3. Add a doubles/triples endorsement, since placarded loads must be split across multiple trailers
  4. Add a passenger endorsement, because placarded loads are regulated the same way passenger transport is

Why: Hauling any placardable quantity of hazardous materials requires the hazardous materials (H) endorsement, which requires a TSA security threat assessment and fingerprinting.

I already hold a Texas Class A CDL and a valid DOT medical certificate. My new employer wants me to start pulling placardable loads of corrosives, so I need to add the H endorsement. When I ask the DPS clerk what extra step this endorsement requires that my current license did NOT, which of the following is she MOST likely to tell me?

  1. Nothing extra is needed; once you hold a Class A CDL with a current DOT medical card, the H endorsement is simply printed on your license at no additional requirement
  2. You must pass a second, more detailed DOT physical and submit an updated medical examiner's certificate before the endorsement can be issued
  3. You must pass a TSA security threat assessment, which includes a background check and fingerprinting, before the endorsement can be issued ✓ Correct answer
  4. You only need to retake and pass the standard CDL vision screening to confirm you can still read placards at a distance

Why: The HazMat endorsement requires passing a TSA security threat assessment, including a background check and fingerprinting, before it can be issued.

I'm hired to haul a load of corrosive material and I'm reviewing the paperwork at the dock before departure. I notice the hazard class, proper shipping name, and UN identification number are already entered on the shipping papers, but I want to be sure the right party handled that. Under the HazMat rules, whose job was it to identify the material as hazardous and prepare those shipping papers in the first place?

  1. The motor carrier, since the carrier owns the trip and is responsible for transporting the load and reporting any incidents
  2. The driver, since I am the one who must keep the papers within reach and refuse the load if anything looks wrong
  3. The receiver, since the company accepting delivery confirms the contents and signs for the hazardous material
  4. The shipper, since the shipper classifies, names, packages, and labels the material and prepares the shipping papers ✓ Correct answer

Why: The shipper classifies, names, packages, and labels the hazardous material and prepares the shipping papers before the load reaches the driver.

I'm hauling a placarded load of corrosives across Texas and just merged onto the interstate. To stay compliant with HazMat shipping-paper rules while I'm driving, where should the shipping papers be right now?

  1. Within my reach with my seat belt on, or in a pouch on the driver's door, and on top of my other papers ✓ Correct answer
  2. Secured in the sleeper berth so wind from an open window can't scatter them
  3. Filed in the document box mounted on the outside of the trailer near the placards
  4. Tucked under the freight in the trailer so they stay with the actual cargo

Why: While the vehicle is moving, HazMat shipping papers must be within the driver's reach with the seat belt on, or in the driver's door pouch, and on top of other papers.

I'm a new driver loading a mixed freight trailer that includes one HazMat item along with several non-regulated commodities. When I review the combined shipping paper before I leave the dock, how should the HazMat material be identified on that document so the paperwork is legal?

  1. Entered last on the page so it stays separate from the ordinary freight lines
  2. Entered first, or highlighted, or marked with an 'X' in the HM column ✓ Correct answer
  3. Written only on a separate back page that is stapled behind the freight list
  4. Left in regular print since the placards on the trailer already flag the hazard

Why: On a mixed shipping paper, the HazMat entry must be made distinguishable by entering it first, highlighting it, or marking it with an 'X' in the HM column.
