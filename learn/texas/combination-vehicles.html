Sample questions

You are driving a 53-foot tractor-trailer and approaching a tight right turn at a city intersection. As you begin to steer the tractor through the turn, where will your trailer's rear wheels track relative to the path your front (tractor) wheels are following?

  1. The rear wheels will track a tighter, inner path than the front wheels, so I must swing wide to let the trailer clear the curb. ✓ Correct answer
  2. The rear wheels will track a wider, outer path than the front wheels, so I should hug the inside of the turn.
  3. The rear wheels will follow the exact same path as the front wheels, so I can turn as if driving a straight truck.
  4. The rear wheels will not follow any predictable path, so I should brake hard mid-turn to stay in control.

Why: The trailer's rear wheels track a tighter inner path than the front wheels (off-tracking), so swing wide enough for the trailer to clear the corner.

I'm driving a tractor-trailer and need to make a tight right turn at a city intersection. I know my trailer's rear wheels off-track and follow a tighter path than my steer axle, and I want to keep the trailer from climbing the curb. What is the BEST way to steer through this turn?

  1. Swing the tractor wide to the left first to open up room, then complete the turn
  2. Keep the rear of the trailer close to the curb and swing wide only as you finish the turn ✓ Correct answer
  3. Begin turning the wheel sharply and early so the whole rig pivots before reaching the corner
  4. Drift toward the center line ahead of the corner so the trailer has space to cut inside

Why: Because of off-tracking, keep the trailer's rear near the curb and swing wide only as you complete the turn so it clears the corner.

I'm doing a pre-trip inspection on my tractor-trailer and I trace the air hoses running from the back of the cab to the front of the trailer. As I follow them to the glad-hand connectors, how many air lines should I expect to find joining the two units?

  1. Three lines: a service line, an emergency line, and a separate ABS line
  2. One line, since a single supply hose feeds both the service and emergency systems
  3. Two lines: the service (control) line and the emergency (supply) line ✓ Correct answer
  4. Four lines, one for each pair of glad-hand connectors on the units

Why: A tractor and trailer are joined by exactly two air lines: the service (control) line and the emergency (supply) line.

During my pre-trip, I'm connecting the two air lines between my tractor and trailer at the glad-hands. I know one line carries the service (control) signal and the other is the emergency (supply) line that charges the trailer tanks and applies the spring brakes if its air is lost. To make sure I don't cross them, which color glad-hand should I match to the emergency (supply) line?

  1. Blue, because the supply line that charges the trailer tanks is the higher-priority line and uses the cool color code
  2. Green, because that line feeds the trailer's emergency air and matches the marker-light circuit on the pigtail
  3. Yellow, because the line that triggers the trailer spring brakes is color-coded as the caution or emergency line
  4. Red, because the emergency (supply) line is red while the service (control) line is blue ✓ Correct answer

Why: The glad-hands are color coded: red is the emergency (supply) line and blue is the service (control) line.

I'm doing my pre-trip on a tractor-trailer and I push the red, eight-sided knob on the dash all the way in. Then I get out and walk back to check the rig. What did pushing that red knob just do?

  1. It opened the trailer air supply, charging the trailer tanks and releasing the trailer's spring (emergency) brakes so the rig can move ✓ Correct answer
  2. It set the tractor parking brake by applying the spring brakes on the drive axle
  3. It applied the trailer service brakes through the blue control line for a brake test
  4. It released the tractor parking brake so only the tractor can roll forward

Why: Pushing in the red trailer air supply knob charges the trailer tanks and releases the trailer's spring (emergency) brakes so the rig can move.

I'm hauling a loaded trailer down a rural Texas highway when the trailer suddenly breaks away and a coupling failure causes a major air leak at the back of my rig. As the air rushes out, what is the tractor protection valve designed to do for me in this exact moment?

  1. Automatically apply the trailer's spring brakes so the runaway trailer stops itself
  2. Close off the lines to keep enough air in the tractor system so I keep brakes on the power unit ✓ Correct answer
  3. Re-pressurize the trailer air tanks through the emergency line so I can recouple safely
  4. Lock the fifth wheel jaws tighter around the kingpin to stop the trailer from separating

Why: If trailer air pressure drops too low, the tractor protection valve closes off the lines to keep enough air in the tractor system for braking the power unit.
